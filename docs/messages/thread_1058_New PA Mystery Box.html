From: nattybumppolbna | Date: 2005-05-31 01:44:08 UTC
Wow - that sounds impressive. But isn't that part of the joy of doing the thing? That you
make this wonderful thing and let it go off into the the uncertain world? Maybe that's just
me and my letterboxing values.

Don't we all put a tremendous amount of time in on these boxes? Maybe not all of them,
of course, the world needs drive-bys, too. But is a box better or more special because we
pay for it? I may not have cool logbooks, but I've spent days planning and walking-off
clues. I've been working on Murder By Numbers for months. I'm staring down coyotes
now, too. If we're going to be paying for them, what are the criteria for the "better" boxes?

There are two things that are sticking in my craw on this.

1. Newbies got credit cards, too. Ability to pay is no gauge of letterboxing ability,
courtesy, or common sense. I know a guy who is a snowboarding instructor. He says he
always knows who will be taken off the mountain in the ambulance. It's usually the guy
who is wearing the brand new expensive snowboarding clothes and is carrying the most
expensive snowboard made - to his first lesson.

2. If I am paying for the clue, don't you owe something? Like a guarantee the box is
going to be there? What am I getting for my cash? A peek at precious prose?

I don't want to sound like I am ranting against this. I haven't made up my mind yet. It's a
pretty simple thing to set up a commercial letterboxing site. Heck, Yahoo will do it for you
- just add clues. I guess we could all do that for our clues, if the game goes that way. But
as Luke, Leia, Han, Obi-Wan and, I suspect, Chewbacca have all said, "I've got a bad feeling
about this."

Re: New PA Mystery Box/Discussion Question

From: rscarpen | Date: 2005-05-31 06:02:25 UTC
> Is this the future of letterboxing? Pay-to-play? Will we
> eventually be registering our credit cards on sites with monthly
> fees like porn sites? [So I hear.]

Premium Member Only clues are provided as a service to those who'd
like to restrict access to certain boxes, and the concept wasn't
initially even my idea. I was chatting with others who didn't want
*everyone* looking for certain boxes and ways to limit access. WOM
clues are the defacto way of doing that. Some people make really
crypic clues to reduce the numbers. I prefer putting my boxes on
hellishly long hikes to discourage people. (Or encourage them to go
for a real hike, depending on your point of view!) I've just provide
yet another option in the form of Premium Member Only boxes. Now you
don't have to create complex clues to scare people off nor do they
have to keep giving the clue out directly to people who might be
interested.

I've spent thousands of hours and hundreds of dollars developing Atlas
Quest. Premium Membership was just a method to help compensate me for
the time and money I've invested into it, but I believe that
letterboxing--at its most basic--should be free. By default, all clues
on AQ are freely available. In most cases, there's no reason why
anyone should ever have to pay for a clue--there's almost always a
letterbox available with a freely available clue you can find instead.

There are only about 50 letterboxes listed on Atlas Quest that require
premium membership--out of over 6,000. Less than 1%. It's not a
money-maker by any stretch of the imagination. Most boxes listed as
PMOs are listed that way because the person who planted it only wants
"serious letterboxers" looking for them. Those who are serious enough
about the hobby that they are willing to help pay for improvements. Or
at least cut out the "fly-by-nighters" who might be less inclined to
replace a box as well as it should be replaced.

It sounds to me like some people find it offensive that they can't
have all the clues immediately accessible at all times. Some people
get twisted out of shape about WOM clues for the same reason. They
don't want to have "connections" to get clues--they just want them
handed out, for free, on websites that other people pay for out of
their own pockets. LbNA is a pretty good gig--hosting that website
isn't free, and the people who run it are paying for it out of their
own pockets. And have been for years. It's remarkable--it's rare to
find such a selfless website and I think sometimes people forgot how
unusual such a setup really is.

In any case, I've designed Atlas Quest with the intention that
letterboxing is more-or-less a free hobby, and it will always stay
that way. Over 99% of the clues are still freely accessible, and the
ones that aren't were set up that way by the person who listed the
clue--not Atlas Quest. If you don't have access to 1% of the clues, so
what? Look for the other 99% of the boxes that are listed. Keep in
mind, though, on Atlas Quest, it's the premium members that are paying
for you being able to access those other 99% of the clues at no
charge. It's the premium members who are paying for improvements to
the site that *everyone* benefits from.

Premium membership has its benefits, but the vast majority of Atlas
Quest is completely accessible by everyone for free. Also keep in mind
that your lunch today probably cost more than a month of premium
membership would. At less than ten cents a day, it's hardly a barrier
to anyone who decides they really want a clue bad enough. And if you
don't, you have that option--look for the other 99% of boxes that are
listed instead.

Amanda wanted to place a few Premium Member Only boxes as a thank you
to those who have supported Atlas Quest because she knows first hand
the amount of effort and money that goes into it. As a thank you to
premium members, it's sensible to make the clue available to *only*
premium members. After all, that is who the boxes are intended for!

Now as Mark would say, get out there and box. There's plenty of clues
available regardless of whether or not you're a premium member on AQ. =)

-- Ryan

From: samanark | Date: 2005-06-05 00:03:54 UTC
One of the great things about letterboxing is that you don't have to
pay much to play. Gas money to and from the trailhead, a journal and a
stamp and you are good to go.

All this extra stuff in letterboxing is not neccessary. You don't have
to participate in the talk lists, you don't have to plant boxes, you
don't have to go to gatherings. You don't even have to own a computer.
You can go to the library and get 1000's of clues for free online and
go hunt for them. Or you can go to a local EMS or other outdoor store
that provides clues to local boxes. You can organize your own "private
letterboxing cabal" among your local church youth group or scouting
organization. You don't have to rely on Atlas Quest or LBNA for your
clues. It just depends on what you want to do with the hobby.

Do you like to play soccer? You can play soccer without paying for a
bunch of "commercialization". Just call up all your friends and go to
the park and play soccer. OR you can join a league (costs money) wear
a uniform (costs money and often advertises a local pizza place or
sporting goods store) and set a schedule, have playoffs and
tournaments with other teams. It depends on how you like to play
soccer. (How serious are you about your soccer??)

I don't mind a little commercialization (well, I was the one who
started all this with my plug for premium member boxes!) I like
letterboxing T shirts and backpacks and patches and wristbands. I love
all the stuff that Silent Doug has for sale at Cafe Press! I wish I
could afford to buy a ton of it. I love finding letterboxes by great
carvers like Spring Chick and Art Trekker and She Who Plants and I do
pay a ton of money in traveling fees (airline fares and rental cars,
hotels and camping fees, fees to park the car and fees to enter
parks!) to find these letterboxes. And I have paid to find boxes that
weren't so fantastic, that I found were missing or damaged. In many
cases, where I had to pay a fee to enter a park...to find one
letterbox, I would add a box in the same park, just to make it "more
worthwhile" for the next letterboxer. Now there are two letterboxes in
that park.

Sometimes I have bypassed a letterbox because there was a fee to enter
the park. (I'm on a budget!)
Again, it is all about what you want to do with the hobby. If you
don't want to spend a lot of money, you can do that and you can still
get some wonderful letterboxes by some wonderful letterboxers. The
amount of money you spend doesn't mean a thing. Some folks spend a
fortune on their letterboxes with expensive rubbermaid containers and
ink pads, art paper pads and special stamps professionally made! And
other folks carve an eraser and get their paper and containers from
the dollar store. Either way can yield a fantastic, artistic
letterbox. Again, it is how YOU want to play the game.

-Amanda from Seattle

It seems to me, in this game without rules, that we operate more like a small village. We
don't have laws, we have mores, customs and taboos. We never say you can't do things.
We discourage them as a group. We discourage burying boxes. We discourage placing
boxes in sensitive areas. We encourage boxes for families and people with limited
physical abilities. Letterboxing is what we say it is.

Dartmoor letterboxing has survived for over 100 years because of the secrecy of the
participants and a limited area of practice. For us, that ship has sailed. In just 7 years we
have more than doubled the number of letterboxes and exported the game all over the
world.

Change is a slow, gradual process where you wake up one morning in your farmhouse to
find a McDonald's next door.

Not all change is bad. Do you control it? Or does it control you? Granted AtlasQuest is a
great site run by great people. What about the next one?
