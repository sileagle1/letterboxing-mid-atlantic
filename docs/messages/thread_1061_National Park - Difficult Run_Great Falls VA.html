I'm going to bring this here rather than get in the flame war on the
main list.

Here is a second post from the geocaching forum:
"Retired NPS ranger here with a word of advice. Contact the owner of
the letterbox and have him remove it pronto. He'd be liable for a
hefty fine if it was discovered, and it wouldn't take the Fed more
than five minutes to find out his real name, his great-grandmother's
address in 1916 and what he had for breakfast this morning."

My sixth sense tells me that these guys really are going to call the
park and make trouble, just to prove that they are right. Another
possibility is that NPS personnel will discover these by reading the
geocaching.com forums themselves next week.

I've dealt with Great Falls for orienteering, they'll be professional
about it, but they aren't going to ignore these letterboxes having
been thrown in their faces in the manner that "Difficult Run" has
done. The fact that the clues didn't list Great Falls may have given
them some cover to live and let live, or maybe they had completely
missed the boxes on their land, but that's blown now, or it will be
when the busybodies hit the phone.

Unless the owners of the boxes in question have permission in writing
from the park itself (unfortunately I doubt that, based on the park),
they may want to grab the boxes, for no other reason than to preserve
the letterboxes from the muggles that are coming.

If anyone else has one hidden on other NPS land in the Capitol Region
(or maybe NPS land overall), the same advice applies, based on the
fact that parks do talk to each other. We may even wind up in Morning
Report.

Sorry to be contrarian here, but I see this deal hitting the fan.

Mike
