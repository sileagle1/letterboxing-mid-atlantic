I'm new to both letterboxing and this list so I need a bit of
advise. I've sent mail to five placers for various reasons in the
past six weeks and haven't received a response from anyone. I suppose
the most troubling was the report of a missing box and no alert on
the clue list.

I'll elaborate. I sent mail to the owner of the very first box that I
found. OK, I was excited. I sent mail to the placer of the second
box that I found because there was a first finders 'prize'. OK, I was
still excited. I sent mail to the placer of an animal chewed box. I
didn't have much with me but I dried everything out as well as I
could and put the box back holes down so that it hopefully wouldn't
get wet again. Then, there was the missing box. The area wasn't far
from my home, so I went to look for it twice before reporting it
lost. No response from owner; nothing on the clue list. Next, a
swamped box. Soaked. I left the area and went to a store and bought a
small notebook to replace the wet one. The book was getting moldy so
I took it with me to dry it out. I contacted placer to ask if they
wanted me to double/triple bag it and put it back outside of the box,
mail it or just get rid of it because it was smeared and moldy.
Nothing.

Are people simply wary of unknown new letterboxers? I'm a 46 year old
NJ housewife but I suppose that I might seem like a threatening,
unknown entity in email. Have some of these people, perhaps, stopped
letterboxing and don't care any more? Do they have two or three email
addresses and don't often check the one that was given as a response
site on the clue list? or(D) all of the above. Kidding there. Any
thoughts on this matter would be appreciated.

Gillian

From: Flame Thrower | Date: 2004-05-27 02:53:51 UTC
Perhaps they have grown fat and lazy like me. For the first 6
months you are so buzzed by the excitement that you skip out of work
grinning like an idiot wondering if you can nab 5 boxes in 45
minutes before the sun goes down. It doesn't last though.

You will hit a saturation point where you mellow on the hobby. It
will be around the time that you have 60 or so boxes and you just
can't read another flame war or your head will explode.

I've hung around because some of the people are amazing and the
dream of a sublime box is still a draw.

The point of this walk down memory lane is:

1. People don't check their e-mail daily.
2. Some people have dropped out and when they go, it is with a
whimper and not a bang. (although at least one person has been
kicked off of the Yahoo site)
3. They don't care that the box is missing.
4. Maybe they are a little afraid of you.
5. They're fat and lazy like me. (see, it all comes full circle!)

My advice is to shake it off and move on. Find what you can, lament
about the rest, and don't take it too seriously or the fun suckers
will milk you dry.

Tammy

Hi Gillian,

A couple of things might have happened, other than the placers just
not bothering to reply for every box. Although that does happen!

-The first is if you used the "contact the placer" function on the
clue page at letterboxing.org, and if the box owner replied to your
email without checking the return address....then letterbox.org got
your reply. It took me a looong time to figure this out, in fact Wes
finally had to tell me (a big D'oh! here). :-)

-Maybe they didn't recognise the email as being letterbox related. My
email program filters out spam and sometimes "good" mail gets dumped
too. I try to double check what isn't obviously spam but sometimes I
just don't have time, or forget. For this reason I put "Letterboxing"
or something similar in the subject line when asking about a box.

Hope this is helpful.
Cheers,
catbead

In addition to all the things I have learned in the de-cyphering of
clues, e.g. the definition of "sublime", I am intrigued by the pocket
parks in NoVA I never knew existed, and I do like the artwork I am
collecting (so prefer a carved stamp of any caliber), which one day
may be as valuable as the Tramp Art shown on Antiques Road Show (one
has to plan for retirement!). So, in order of sublimity I would say
(1) cleaver clues that require internet research or tell me a story
with facts I didn't know, amusing is also a plus even before I leave
the house(office!) (2)at the 'site', clues which pick up on specifics
of the area beyond "double trunk tree" or make me use my compass (3)a
great piece of art that requires at least three colors to enhance (4)
having fantastic planters with which to gather--guess that one is
outside the box.

From: fleurdelislb | Date: 2004-05-28 01:48:53 UTC
I've never really thought of it that way - to me each box is a lot of
fun even if little effort is put into it.

But I guess a "sublime" box would contain a well-done hand-carved
stamp and a handmade logbook. The location it brought me to would be
beautiful and new to me. I agree with what SewSow/Judy said, that I
do enjoy reading about the area I'm in so if history is provided,
that's a plus. I think some of the more fun boxes I've hunted were
creatively written, such as Pico's poem for "Boards and Bridges" or
the clues for "A Quaker Yarn" by Space Traveller.
