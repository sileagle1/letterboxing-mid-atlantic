From: Mike Brooks | Date: 2005-07-20 09:03:01 UTC
http://www.destateparks.com/geocache/

Delaware is requiring permits for geocaches and letterboxes.

Caches in Lums Pond and Killens Pond, as well as the Mapsurfer #8
letterbox on Pea Patch Island, were the problems cited. For
background, check geocaching.com fourms (search for posts over the
past year by NappingBears, a state park employee), and posts in the
Delaware Geocaching group on groups.msn.com.

Killing the messenger should be avoided.

Mike

Re: Permits for Delaware State Parks

From: Mike Brooks | Date: 2005-07-21 00:23:45 UTC
Let's start with a direct quote from one of the caching posts.

"Please feel free to contact me if you have questions or comments --
I'll keep an eye on the board here, or if you prefer, my e-mail at
work is christine.polo@..., phone is (302) 739-9177."

I've been following this a little over the past year through the
caching forum. While having a policy like this isn't ideal, things
could be worse. Last year, two park managers (Lums Pond and Killens
Pond) requested that all caches in their parks be pulled completely
with the intention of banning the activity. Ms. Polo (who has logged
30 cache finds online and can therefore be considered one of us, or
at least one of them) asked within the central parks office about an
overall policy, and was eventually assigned to the committee that
drafted the policy.

As far as the multiple access part of this, the parks' concern is the
creation of spur or social trails. The theory is to get geocaches and
letterboxes farther off the trail. This quote from the caching forum
says it better than I can: "It's been my experience that caches 30
feet off a main trail are more likely to develop a social trail via
the clearest means of access. A cache 200 feet from a trail will
likely never be accessed by exactly the same route by any two
finders. This also cuts down on the risk of the cache being
discovered accidentally." My experience is identical.

This applies to letterboxes too. Even if the last clue is a pace and
bearing from a specific spot, the farther you go from the trail, the
more that the various finders' paths will disperse, as finders drift
offline here and there, or go around obstacles in different
directions. Spur trails were the reason that the two original park
managers sought to ban our hobbies altogether, and it's the one part
of this that should be taken most seriously.

As far as the logo being added to the online clues, that seems
trivial and I suspect that if this is brought to Ms. Polo's
attention, that a text equivalent would be accepted. Someone might
ask about exceptions for mysteries, such as not posting the fee and
hunting info (still get the permit) to avoid spoiling a mystery. At
the same time, keep in mind that a park staff might treat a true
mystery different than something called a mystery for the purpose of
circumventing a rule.

Based on the posts on the caching forum, no caches nor letterboxes
seem to have been removed yet. They intend to give several weeks
(maybe through the end of the peak summer season?) for everyone to
apply, then start chasing down owners of caches/boxes not in
compliance. Having said that, you might ask if a certain missing box
had caused a specific problem and was removed based on that problem.

That's the long version of what I've picked up from the caching
forums. Happy letterboxing.

Mike
