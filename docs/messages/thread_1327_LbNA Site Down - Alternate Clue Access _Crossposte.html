LbNA Site Down - Alternate Clue Access (Crossposted)

From: SpringChick | Date: 2005-09-25 18:12:47 UTC
There is currently a problem with access to the LbNA web site.  As far as I am aware, everyone is getting the error -- nobody has been individually denied access as the error message might seem to indicate.  I have not been able to contact Choi so there is no estimate as to possible availability time.
 
If you were hoping to grab some clues and go letterboxing today, here are a couple of alternate suggestions:
 
AtlasQuest - http://www.atlasquest.com - many people list their boxes at both LbNA and AtlasQuest. If the box is listed at AtlasQuest and the clue is stored at AtlasQuest or on the user's personal web site, you will be able to access the clue.  If the box is listed at AtlasQuest, but the clue is stored on the LbNA site, you will receive an error message when trying to access the clue similar to the one received when trying to access the LbNA site.  At this point, you can click on "Cached Clue" at the far right of the header menu to see a cached version of the clue.  Be aware that the cached version is not up-to-date as the clues as cached monthly, but if the clue has not changed recently, this should be fine.  Also note that the cached version will contain only text, not graphics, so if there were pictures as a part of the clue, you are out of luck.
 
Old LbNA Site - http://lbna.dyndns.org/index.html - if a box is not listed on AtlasQuest and it is an older letterbox, you may be able to find the clue at the old LbNA web site.  Again, these clues are not up-to-date as changes have not been made since the new site went up 2 years ago, but if you are looking for a specific older box that has not had a change of clue, you might try this.
 
Personal Web Sites - Many letterboxers keep their clues on their personal web sites.  There are lists of personal letterboxer web sites in the links directory at the LbNA Yahoo! list, at many of the regional Yahoo! lists and at Silent Doug's web site (http://www.letterboxing.info/links/?id=14).  Perhaps a 'boxer in your area has a personal site with their box clues and you could get them directly from their web site.
 
Sorry for the inconvenience and thanks for your patience!
 

SpringChick
(one of the webmasters)

This Weekend's Boxing

From: Chile-Pepper | Date: 2005-09-26 02:14:15 UTC
Well, unfortunately the boxing gods were working against us this
weekend, at least a little. We took an impromptu trip and decided to
spend the last day (today) letterboxing. Well, duh, of COURSE we packed
our boxing stuff!!!
Anyway, we couldn't log into LBNA from the hotel, which I wanted to
blame ON the hotel, which I now realize I couldn't do. So no clues from
there.
I did the next best thing; logged into the Yahoo groups for new
announcements and scrolled (and scrolled, and scrolled, and scrolled...)
to find Virginia placements. We finally settled on the Waller's Mill
boxes just north of Williamsburg off I-64. This is a beautiful little
park and the clues were wonderfully clear and concise. EEK had very
little trouble following the clues (we had none) and all four boxes were
in great condition, with the exception of some pretty liquefied stamp
pads (best bet: bring your own).
The only bad side is that I am now a dreaded "gray hole" postal
letterboxer, but I hope to remedy that tomorrow.
ChilePepper

Me, the Authorities & Palmyra Cove....

From: Silent Doug | Date: 2005-09-26 13:16:07 UTC
So Clueless and I were at Palmyra Cove yesterday for an event that
she was leading for her hiking club. While the introductory talk was
going on inside the nature center, I decided to wander out and check
on What Exit. It was productive, in that I found a hitchhiker in the
box, and I noted that the log is filled up and needs to be replaced
(I didn't have anything that would fit inside the box to replace it myself).

About 10 minutes later, though, I noticed the two staff members from
the center hustling through the building with a police officer, and
they were joined by a third policeman outside as they headed down
towards the box hiding place. I watched as they poked around and
eventually found the letterbox, so I figured out what was going on
and headed outside to explain. I told them that it was a letterbox --
part of a "scavenger hunt" that was held in the center last year with
the center's permission -- and I had just checked on it. While I was
careful that there was no one around or in the center looking my way
when I pulled out the box, I didn't notice that there were people on
the observation deck atop the center. One woman saw me put something
in a strange place and alerted the center staff, who called the
police (the station is right next door), and they came out to check
it. One of the police officers recalled the "scavenger hunt" from
last year (probably from the great bounty of leftover food that we
dropped off at the police station after the event!), so they accepted
my explanation and replaced the box, and then headed over to reassure
the woman that she in fact had not thwarted a terrorist plot directed
at a small nature park in southern New Jersey (which surely is among
the top targets for just such activity along the eastern seaboard)....

Note to self -- after too many recent close calls with boxes in
conspicuous hiding places, avoid such letterboxes completely in the future....

SD

From: Rick in Boca | Date: 2005-09-26 18:55:15 UTC
This reminds me of my encounter with the authorities a couple of
weeks ago. It was on 9/11.

I was scoping out a location to place my new Baltus Roll letterbox,
(named after the farmer who owned the property on which the Baltusrol
golf club is located). Baltusrol recently hosted the 2005 PGA
Championship, and security was way elevated during that event.

I hiked in the state-owned area nearby, as Baltusrol is locked up
tighter than a Rubbermaid Seal'n Save. When I exited the forest, a
city cop was waiting for me.

He wanted to know why I had maps of Springfield, and satellite photos
of Baltusrol. He wanted to know what was in my trunk. In my backpack.
In the bags in my backpack. He was not convinced I was just hiking.

At one point he pointed to all the baggies and asked me if I am OCD.

How do you explain Letterboxing to a city cop?

I started with GeoCaching, hoping maybe he had heard of it. Nope.

Then I sighed, and started trying to explain how there are clues and
a log book. He says, so this is some kind of innernet thing? Well,
no, see there is this letterbox; and then the cop's younger partner
interrupted and said, "oh, like in Letterboxing dot org?"

So finally they let me go, satisfied that I wasn't going to plant a
bomb set to go off in another 11 months, when they host the next
Championship.
