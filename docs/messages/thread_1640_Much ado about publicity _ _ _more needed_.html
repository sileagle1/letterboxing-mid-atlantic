From: gallant_rogue | Date: 2006-03-26 14:10:26 UTC
I checked out the Philadelphia Inquirer article and it looks very
positive. It will be interesting to see if there is an influx of new
people as a result. Recalling the delayed negative reaction to the
National Public Radio spot I'm curious if there are complaints
looming about this recent publicity. I wonder if we don't need
another such article in the Washington Post. In my humble opinion
there seems a disproportionate letterboxing population around the
Northern Virginia area. At last Fall's "Mystery... A Fall Gather"
held in our own NoVa backyard, the Va/Md/DC area letterboxers were
greatly outnumbered by letterboxers who travelled 100 miles or more
to be there... more than two-to-one.

Is it just me? I don't get a sense that letterboxing is "thriving"
here. There seems a very small number of people active. The few that
are active are placing lots of good letterboxes. But many of these
boxes sit unfound for months or years. It seems just a very few
people
are placing and finding each others letterboxes. Is this healthy? On
the other hand, I literally stumble over geocaches & geocachers all
the time.

gallant_rogue <gallant_rogue@...> wrote: "but I would like for my local boxes to get found more frequently... (I think Bulldawg would too)"
 
Not true...I have changed my whole letterboxing style the past 2-3 months, and prefer as little traffic to my boxes as possible, by not posting any of my clues on AQ or LBNA.  I have found that many fast boxes get traffic soon after the plant then goes dead.  After a few experiences retrieving long hike or difficult boxes, that made me realize that there need to be more challenging boxes.  I feel those boxes reward sooo much more for 3 reasons: 1) It is a personal accomplishment to make such a hike 2) It is often rewarding to get a stamp, that no-one has bothered to find in 1 or 2 years 3) With the less traffic, you are more less likely to hike and find a missing box.

From: June | Date: 2006-03-26 16:20:39 UTC
I'm not going to get into the whole publicity thing because I've already made my feelings known and don't feel the need to keep repeating myself, but as far as boxes sitting unfound for months or years is concerned, one also has to take into account each box, the clues, the location. Some boxers make their boxes hard to get to, or make the clues difficult because they don't ::want:: a lot of traffic. I can see "many" boxes going unfound for months, but are there really "many" that go unfound for years? I don't know. I just don't get that feeling. Mind you, this is coming from someone who doesn't get out of NYC to go boxing anywhere else often enough to know your area. But I just don't get that feeling.
 
I see that there are now 2,808 members on the USA list and though I make a point to stay away from it most of the time, when I do look at it, there are always new members. ::::::::Always:::::::::, without exception. When I found out about boxing in April of 2004, there were I think about 1600. That's growth. Growth is healthy. The people I know and read posts from on all the groups are enthusiastic about boxing. This is healthy. I think the hobby is alive and well and growing. I don't know if you can define the health or lack of it by boxing traffic in one area in any one given period of time.
 
"William County...thats eight letterboxers out of nearly two
million in population"
 
I just saw your new note come up and looked at it. I don't think you can judge the health of the hobby by comparing the number of boxes to the area's population of two million. I don't think the health of letterboxing should be defined by it's size or lack of it, by numbers or lack of them. I think it needs to be defined by the quality of the people who box, they way they box, and the fact that it is indeed growing. And if you take two million and say that you want.............I don't know, hundreds or thousands of people out boxing on a given weekend in one area,  I think the quality of the hobby will suffer greatly.
 
As long as boxing is growing, and I think it's pretty obvious it is, for whatever reason, publicity, WOM or whatever, I think that indicates health. I could compare the small number of boxes here, most of which are mine, to our population of over 8 million, but I don't think it applies. I think boxing is doing just fine. After all, size isn't always important. <eg>

From: hurdhut@aol.com | Date: 2006-03-26 17:25:33 UTC
GR, Though I love the traffic to my boxes, I have no expectations of that to continue. I think all boxers have a different "view" as to what they want out of the hobby. I have different traffic expectations to my local boxes than I do to my more "extreme" boxes. Like Bulldawg, I prefer longer more physically challenging boxes than shorter "kid friendly ones" if I am boxing alone. But, because I hike regularly, what I view as a long, challenging hike for a box will differ from Bulldawg's perspective. If I am with the family, I keep it to shorter boxes, but also have to stay close by the house to keep a 3 and 5 year old from losing complete interest before I even get to a box a county or two away. Some are really into their PFX count, others, like myself, no longer count. It's all good.
    I think what you are saying, is that you want more frequent traffic to your boxes.  Many share that feeling. This is what can cause frustration in the hobby at times. Think of it like fishing...every once in a while you'll be interrupted by actually catching one. I just don't think an influx of new boxers is the answer. At best, you would get a temporary rush in traffic then have it fade again. The problem would be that the influx could lead to boxers with poor habits and etiquette due to a rush to get active in the hobby.
   I don't think you should question if you are "on target." The hobby evolves because we post our thoughts on how it is working. I used to be frustrated with how the hobby was gaining in popularity. I worked that out by just adapting my boxes to how I want it played. I have some that are going with the flow, and others that...well, they are less so. Keep the faith Rogue, the hobby is growing....faster than some of us want. I bid you all safe trails.
                                                           Koyote
