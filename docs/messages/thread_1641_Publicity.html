From: nattybumppolbna | Date: 2006-03-26 18:14:06 UTC
I saw the Inquirer article as well. It was thoughtful, respectful, and well-written. I have no
desire to flame any of the fine 'boxers involved with it.

I would like to use the opportunity to re-voice my concern about letterboxing and
publicity. I am not worried that thousands of noxers will destroy our boxes. I am not
worried that "the wrong kind of people" will begin letterboxing.

I am worried about the people who run the parks where we do most of our work. We
operate under the radar of most people and that includes the people who manage the
State and local parks. Most are either ignorant of our existence or treat us with a kind of
benign neglect - no harm, no foul as long as we treat the parks with respect. I am
concerned that publicity will provoke someone in the bureaucracy to take some sort of
action.

All it takes is one call from some knucklehead at DCNR to Michael Crowley at Tyler State
Park asking if he has some sort of illegal littering game going on and we will all become
outlaws. It has been my experience with organizations that when they are in doubt about
something, they first say, "No." At that point we all become litterbugs at $300 per box.

Below the radar we can all operate freely. Once we become part of the main stream, the
best we will be able to hope for is a designated "letterboxing area" like the frisbee golf
course at Tyler.

How much fun would that be?


Natty "Chicken Little" Bumppo

I like the 'under-the-radar' aspect of letterboxing too. I think seeking permission sometimes contributes to spoiling the hobby for the LB community.

Some finders feel they must get permission from park managers before looking for a box. This alerts managers to letterboxing  and consequently boxes are removed.

This can also happen when hiders look for permission and alert a park manager to the hobby. Permission is refused and consequently the park staff become aware that there are other boxes planted in the park and remove them.

From: peggysue@dejazzd.com | Date: 2006-12-06 14:12:55 UTC
I think what you're trying to say is that letterboxing is more of a covert operation. Broadcasting the existence of boxes, local or remote, can attract "the wrong element," i.e. people who would damage, destroy, or remove boxes for fun. Fortunately, these sorts of people drop out of their variation of the activity pretty quickly, like those who read about it in magazines often do. But telling the whole town, or even region, by way of a newspaper article may be more publicity than many of your local boxers would like. Boxers like to be secretive, esoteric, romantically vague. I agree that holding a local gather of folks already involved is a better way to start out. Getting and keeping the local boxers active and planting will get you more safe boxes to find than telling anyone and everyone that there are secret things out there they can seek and destroy.
PeggySue

RE: [lbma] Re: Publicity

From: June | Date: 2006-12-06 16:00:37 UTC
I also feel that when it comes to boxing, the expression too much a good thing can apply. I think that it is possible to reach a saturation point brought on by "inbreeding", so to speak. (lots of people in a given area planting, planting, planting) I don't see the fun in having so many  boxes in an area that it will take very little time or effort to find some ........because there are so many of them. Where is the fun or challenge in that? It's pretty obvious that letterboxing is growing in leaps and bounds because of the publicity it's already received.

And as much as I love boxing and would like to be able to have more nearer to me so I can go more often without a long drive,  I'm not going to e-mail the lifestyles editor of a local newspaper and suggest they do an article. I'll just do the driving. When I :::do:::: go and when I ::::do:::: find them, I have a lot more fun and get a lot more satisfaction out of it than if I had a ton of them within a few miles of my house. Even though I live in NYC, I live so far north that I could approach a Westchester newspaper or one in another area just north of me to try and drum up some "business", but I won't.

I have pretty much given up on expressing my opinion on the subject because it's pretty much a lost cause, but seeing someone say they want to have a gathering and announce it in a newspaper is just too much for me to keep my mouth shut. (I know you mean well my friend and I don't mean to be unfriendly or nasty. I hope you don't see it that way.)

I think one can have a gathering of some kind in a local community center, museum, club, etc and post announcements at that location, the kind of location where people are coming in from outside and looking at a bulletin board (also, don't lots of public parks have enclosed bulletin boards posting events?) and recruit new people (if recruit they must) without going to the media. I'd rather it be something like that and have 5 people show up who are truly, truly  interested rather than announce it in a newspaper and have 50 people who up who are casually interested and might plant one box before they lose interest, or go out and find a couple, re-hide them poorly and then move on to the next hobby that they get into for a few weekends. It's not that I don't want new boxers, I just want new boxers who "get it" and will respect the hobby and treat it with the love and care it deserves.

missmoon

I'm with Miss Moon on this one...and I think I have enough background to give an opinion. I have NEVER approached a paper to advertise a gathering. I have spoken to the newspapers only when they were searching out a spokesperson for local boxing, and even then I preached about the pitfalls of "temporary boxing" and tried to push the unofficial rules of boxing, "leave no trace", "plant better than you found them", "be stealthy" etc etc. I have taught classes before, but only when approached by local museums, libraries and parks departments. They already HAVE people interested in such activities so you aren't pushing it on the possible uninterested.
 
Same goes for the Fargo gathering. The people involved there are people who have already expressed an interest, and attendees are those who are willing to "seed" the area and share the love of this hobby with like-minded individuals. 
 
If you want more people boxing in your area, start planting. They'll come.
