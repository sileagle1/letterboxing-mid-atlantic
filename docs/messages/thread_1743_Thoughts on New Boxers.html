Thoughts on New Boxers

From: nattybumppolbna | Date: 2006-05-29 03:20:42 UTC
I was out with The Walker doing maintenance on a box whe I came accross some Newbies
on the trail. They were loud and obnoxious - possibly drunk - and yelling the word
"letterbox" for all the world to hear. I passed them and then ducked off the trail to follow
them at a discrete distance to see how they would do.

They got lost twice and asked three people for help, telling them about the letterbox in
the process. Eventually they found the box which they waved about and stamped-in right
on the trail with joggers and dog-walkers watching them. They put the box back without
covering it or sealing it and proclaimed their victory to all as they marched out.

This provoked some thoughts I would like to share with the group in hopes of generating
some open discussion.

One of the big fears discussed about doing publicity for letterboxing was that it might
encourage some people to look for boxes just to vandalize them. I don't think that's
nearly as much of a problem as people looking for letterboxes who haven't been taught
the value of BEING COOL ABOUT IT. When word of the game is passed person-to-person
one of the values communicated is often the importance of secrecy in increasing box
survival. I don't think you get that with some kind of mass communication medium.

I am also impressed by the number of letterboxers who play the game without
participating in the groups. I would venture a guess that there might be more people who
box without ever setting foot here or on the big list than there are those of us who
participate in this online community. This is significant to me because I am also
concerned about how the values of letterboxing are transmitted via these online groups.

So let me bring this around to a question to perhaps provoke some discussion. Is there
something we can do to better communicate the (very few) values of letterboxing to
people? Should we even try? Would it help make better letterboxers out there and
improve the game for us all? Am I worrying over nothing?

Discuss, please.

Natt-

BTW I closed up the box and re-hid it properly. B&E you can breathe easy.

From: June | Date: 2006-05-29 09:56:16 UTC
I don't think you are worrying over nothing. I think people like that are a growing problem. My only hope is that they are the ones that don't participate in the hobby for very long. I think one thing each of us can do is ALWAYS point out the importance of secrecy and discreetion (and any other values) when we write up our clues, though most of us already do that, don't we? I don't know if we can make jerks stop being jerks.
 
The question also brings me back to my sore point of publicity. If some jerk reads a newspaper article about boxing and it includes statements about how important it is to be discreet, isn't he or she going to say..........uh.......if you are so concerned about discretion, why do you have all this information and pictures here in the paper for all to read?
 
Maybe the owners of AQ and LBNA (if they don't already-I haven't explored the sites lately) could have, along with all the other rules and regulations they seem to have come up with lately, a very prominent link entitled "The Importance of Discretion In Letterboxing".
 
When it comes down to it, jerks are going to be jerks, no matter what you tell them to do or not to do.
 
Since as someone pointed out to me in the last little "todo" we had about my statement on publicity, the horse is already out of the barn, the only thing each of us can do is always, always, stress discretion in our clues and to anyone who we know has just started boxing. And if we run into people like the ones you saw, (or at least the sober ones) take it upon ourselves to stop them and nicely and politely point out the error of their ways. If we are going to "talk the talk", we should "walk the walk".

Until this past week, we could travel for well over an hour by car without ever coming
across another placer's letterboxes. Because of this, only a dozen or two newbies have
even "tried" letterboxing in my area since I have started planting. None of those who
contacted me said they came across the information because of a printed article, most
were introduced by reading about it somewhere on the internet or by a friend. There were
a couple who found my one box by mistake.

I feel I have a certain amount of responsibility to the folks who start letterboxing in the
general area where I choose to plant. I do my best to respond in a thoughtful way to any
initial communications letting me know my letterbox was found. If I can see they are brand
new (you can usually tell on AQ), I ask if they are local, how they found out about
letterboxing and include a few helpful links for beginners that include FAQs, letterboxing
ethics, and yahoo groups. I try to make it a short note because too much information will
be overwhelming and disregarded.

I know some folks won't agree with the need to return to their boxes unless they get a
note telling them something is wrong but I feel I have a personal responsibility to check on
them regularly. I see who has found my boxes and that they have not been left in a
trashed way that they are ruined and represent letterboxing poorly. Making sure they are
hidden "just so" gives any new boxers clear information on just how a letterbox should
look when they find their very first find. There are a few times I have noticed trends in who
stamped last and who leaves boxes partially open and it is not always newbies. One
newbie and her family, Ammie, have consistently closed and hidden my letterboxes in a
way that I was sure that I was the last one to have been there. I was so surprised to see
this group of 5 stamped into at least 6 of my letterboxes that were re-hidden exactly the
way I would have returned them. I made sure to write an e-mail to them, commending
them for their exact efforts.

Not all folks who try letterboxing can easily be contacted. If I see who I think are a new
family or individual stamped into any of my boxes (they sometimes write "our very first
letterbox!" in the log), I try to research to find them to contact them via e-mail. Some of
these new folks don't even have stamps yet and are trying out letterboxing before
registering a name. One of the research methods I use is looking to see if they have
registered the name on geocaching.com. Another thing I do to communicate with folks
like this (or anyone who accidentally find any of my letterboxes) is to include some
attractive information sheets to encourage them to learn all of the facts and ethics. I
include a few in each letterbox so they feel free to take one with them.

Of those 20 or so newbies whose first finds were any of our letterboxes, maybe 5 have
stuck with it for longer than a few months. I have found them to be thoughtful and
encourage their feedback. A couple of local die hards, bless their souls for putting up with
me, have finally planted a letterbox of their own. It is a quality hide with a hand carved
stamp.

I think if we can treat the newbies of today as if they have the potential to be seasoned
boxers, the letterboxing community far and wide will reap the benefits.

true indigo

Syner and I found our first box on 9 Apr 06, so we're definitely
newbies, and you can take our views for what they may be worth.

We were both serious outside folks before we learned enough about
letterboxing, via the article in the Philadelphia Inquirer, to become
interested. Values such as the "Leave No Trace" ethic were already
important to us.

As we read the postings and find boxes, we learn more about
letterboxing. We haven't met any other boxers yet but that will be
cool when it happens.

To me, a drunken idiot with a letterbox is no more a letterboxer than
a drunken idiot in an art museum is a connisseur. The particular
task occupying the drunken idiot is irrelevant.

Just a quick note to newbies about mid-Atlantic Gatherings:
 
When I attended my first gathering in autumn 2004 (Palmyra Cove!), I was rather anxious about meeting all these other boxers.  I wondered if I would fit in, if I would be overwhelmed by more experienced boxers and in-jokes.  Instead, I had an absolute blast.  Lightnin' Bug immediately took me under his buggy wing, and I met loads of other wonderful people, found lots of boxes, and discovered that this group is really a load of fun.  I've only been to one other gathering since, due to my schedule, but I'm happy to call many other mid-Atlantic boxers friends, and at least one a role model.  (You know who you are!  *wink*) 
 
So, if you're interested, I encourage you to push past the anxiety.  This really is a fantastic group of people.

From: mother_duck_n_brood | Date: 2006-06-04 14:12:09 UTC
Meeting a boxer on the trails was how I got into boxing in the first
place. I was out on a pretty straight-forward kind of trail in a
relatively small park last year, and met a gal with her dad and a
little red wagon in tow, muttering over a piece of paper like they
were totally lost. I asked if I could help them find something and
they explained the whole thing to me. The next week, I was walking
the same trail, clues in hand and kids in tow, to find what they could
not. A month later, I had my first box planted in the same park.

Since then, I have yet to meet anyone on the trails, but maybe
eventually!
