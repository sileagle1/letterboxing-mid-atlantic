PA: Pinnacle and Pulpit Rock boxes restored

From: Silent Doug | Date: 2004-07-12 01:45:32 UTC
Jennifer and I restored the Pinnacle and Pulpit Rock letterboxes in
Hamburg, PA today. These were originally planted in 98-99 by Marty, making
them some of the earliest letterboxes planted in the US, but had fallen
into disrepair in recent years. Now that they've been adopted, the boxes
have new logs and stamps, and the clues have been verified (and adjusted as
necessary). You can find the clues on the LbNA site or at:
http://www.letterboxing.info/silentdoug/planted.php

It's a 9-mile round trip along the Appalachian Trail to get both boxes, but
you'll be rewarded with spectacular views and two letterboxes!

Doug

cahillymom - you were wise not to stop for the Tinicum boxes. Judy
and I stopped for them, in spite of the art show. We followed the
clues to the first box carefully, found the landmark tree on the tow
path, took the compass reading to find THE TREE and then spent about
an hour poking around every darn tree, upright and down, in the disc
golf course. Nada. So we headed in the other direction to find the
other box. We found the creek and bridge and then got stuck. The
clues say go down to the creek bed and follow it. The creek bed may
have been dry when the clues were first posted but you can't get down
there now. So we tried to follow the creek. WHICH DIRECTION!!?? No
clue. AARGH. We first chose the easiest direction which took us
through people's backyards - not in wooded backyards either, we were
on their lawn! This was way too conspicuous so we went back to the
bridge. We thought we'd try in the other direction but we couldn't
even follow the creek in that direction because of the evil looking
underbrush and me, wearing shorts, did not want to rip her legs to
shreds. We cursed H&H and tramped a mile or more back to the car
with nothing to show for it. We were tired and cranky and had to
stop for ice cream. That helped. :)

So, Hikers and Hounds - Have you ever received any confirmation that
these boxes have been found? And you might want to amend the clues
as to the second box - WHICH WAY DO YOU FOLLOW THE CREEK? Do we
really need to walk in backyards? Or is it in the other,
inaccessible direction?

I had a great time this weekend. Thanks, Irene, for everything.

Mary

Re: PA: Pinnacle and Pulpit Rock boxes restored

From: Silent Doug | Date: 2004-07-13 20:55:32 UTC
A couple of extra notes on the Pinnacle and Pulpit Rock letterboxes:

I carved the Pinnacle stamp on the spot -- I wasn't sure that it was
missing, all I knew was that the box was completely water logged. I brought
along everything to restore it (a new box, baggies, log book, a piece of
speedy stamp and my carving knife), so I took a couple of minutes to make a
new stamp for it. If you have the image on the clue page (which is the
original), you can compare my handiwork and grade the results!

On your way up the mountain, pick up a rock and carry it to the top. You'll
know what to do with it when you reach the Pinnacle! (PA is known for
having some of the rockiest sections of the Appalachian Trail....)

From mid-June until mid-August, you'll probably run into Appalachian Trail
thru-hikers on this stretch of trail. This is when they'll be passing
through the area on the 2,174 mile trek from Springer Mountain in Georgia
to Mt. Katahdin in Maine. If you want to see some happy faces, take along
some extra cold sodas, fresh fruit or other goodies and offer them to any
thru-hikers you pass by on the trail (or, if you're like me, it's more
likely that they'll be passing by you even with their 40-lb backpacks!).
There are lots of places along the AT where trail angels traditionally
visit to pass out treats to the committed hikers, and you can be part of
the tradition too. Just ask any dirty, sweaty, tired looking backpackers if
they're thru-hiking and, if so, offer up a cold drink -- and see how
appreciative they are for your generosity!

Finally, don't miss the secret passage at the Pinnacle. Any letterboxer
ought to be able to find it -- and a flashlight just might come in handy.

Doug
