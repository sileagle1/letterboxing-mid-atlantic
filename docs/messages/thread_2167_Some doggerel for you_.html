From: rozebud.rm | Date: 2006-11-28 23:13:32 UTC
In honor of our good friends on the national list, I wrote a little
poem. Geckospot encouraged me to post it here for you fine folks to
enjoy. Here you go!

I made a little letterbox.
The stamp came out so fine.
In great detail I carved it out,
And didn't miss a line.

I made a little letterbox.
The log's a work of art.
I've double-bagged it in the box;
It shouldn't fall apart.

I made a little letterbox.
The hiding place is pretty!
A scenic hike, a county park
Not too far from the city.

I made a little letterbox.
The clues I wrote are hard,
(THAT should keep the noobs away.)
In verse form  I'm a bard!

I made a little letterbox.
Shall I post it on AQ?
But what if someone seeks it out
Who don't know what to do?

I made a little letterbox.
Off-line I'll keep the clues!
So only those who contact me
Will get location news.

What if that's not secure enough?
I'm frightened for it now.
What if the baggie goes unsealed?
What if it's eaten by a cow?

What if a band of newbies
With kids and dogs in tow
Shout out "I found the letterbox!"
For all the world to know?

What if they're more experienced,
But careless just one time?
What if the lid's not put on right
And the box fills up with grime?

I guess I could go postal
And mail it around.
But even then it could get lost!
No, that idea's not sound.

I made a little letterbox.
It's sitting on my shelf.
The world's a big and scary place.
I'll just keep it for myself.

~ rozebud

"Sharing is caring!"

and have shared a lot of fun.
But I like the most, the little notes,
from folks out on the run,

Who say,

We never would have come here otherwise."

I think you nailed it on the head Maryann. I think most here are in the
"Your box, your choice" crowd, but when we create boxes, we actually
want people to find them (well, at least be able to try). The whole
issue, as I see it, is there are a certain few who have boxes, choose to
make them WOM (which is fine), but then say, "well, I have this box, but
YOU are not good enough to have the clues." If that is the case, I
don't want the clues. Screw that, there are plenty of boxes out there,
I don't need that person's box.

This cuts to another issue, that there are a few people out there that
think letterboxing should be how it was at the begining, in '98.

Well, it isn't, that is just the way it is. Does it mean that new boxes
are not clever, thought provoking, fun? No. And if they don't see
that, they are just plain stupid and don't pay attention. When you look
at clues, like say the Bowery Mission boxes (to point one out), you
can't help but be impressed by the work that has gone into them. And
there are a ton more just like that out there.

Boxes come in all shapes, sizes, and difficulties, and that, in my never
humble opinion, is a good thing. I am glad that a family like the
Rosens, who we met last weekend, and go out, have a nice day in the
park with the kids, and find something in the woods, while at the same
time people like the Hairyleggers can be challenged to find the most
difficult of boxes. There is something for everyone, and that is one of
the things that makes it great.
