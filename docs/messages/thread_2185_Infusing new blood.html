Infusing new blood

From: peggysue@dejazzd.com | Date: 2006-12-07 01:20:06 UTC
I was wondering what experiences folks have had with park representatives. We were looking for a box that required a clue from a brass plaque on an historic building. As we were looking at the plaque, a man drove up in a Chevy Blazer with no distinguishing characteristics (neither the man nor the Blazer). He said, "What are you doing there?" I replied, "Who wants to know?" The man identified himself, with official badge and all, as the Park Manager. So since the directions for this particular box said it was placed with permission, we felt comfortable telling him we were looking for the box, and needed a clue from the plaque. He seemed upset and proceeded to say that he wished they hadn't chosed this spot because if people happen to drive by and see people reading the historic marker on the public building, someone would probably come back and steal it. Now correct me if I'm wrong, but aren't these brass markers put there for the education of the public, and we're SUPPOSED to look at them??

Which brings me to my next question. I like the idea of working with park folks to plant a few boxes, and maybe hold some informal events both for seasoned boxers and to introduce new folks to the fun. How do you think this would fly with the park people? If they were cooperative, I think this would be a great way to infuse new boxing blood into an area.

PeggySue

Re: Infusing new blood

From: peggysue@dejazzd.com | Date: 2006-12-07 13:20:22 UTC
I'm so glad I'm not the only one who thought this was a strange reaction. Can't you see all the rubber-neckers on the road stretching their heads out the window to see what those two folks in boxer shorts are looking at? The poor guy would be there all day, "Move along folks. Nothing to see here." All we needed were a couple of numbers from the marker to calculate our bearings.

Another odd thing about this was that once he was satisfied that we weren't prying the brass from the side of the building, he had a lot of interesting facts to offer about the building's history that weren't mentioned on the plaque. I guess he was just initially frustrated that someone had found his secret park, and he had to get out of his car seat to interact with humans.

This brings up that great benefit of boxing - the *general* public has no idea some of these out-of-way public areas exist. It helps you understand how Columbus or Ponce deLeon must have felt when they saw those first waymarkers declaring San Salvador and Florida as historic sites.

PeggySue

From: brendan_hickey13 | Date: 2006-12-07 16:50:03 UTC
It does sound like an odd reaction, but you never know what else had
been happening - maybe just had a round of vandalism or other
nastiness, maybe he was just having a rough day.

In terms of reaching out to parks, I'm sure that other folks have
good ideas and better stories, but I think that it can be delicate.
DE State Parks have an established state-wide procedure for obtaining
approval to plant a box, and it seems like some rangers or supers
surf the Web for unapproved boxes in their parks, so there's much to
be said for being straightforward.

I don't think that PA State Parks have a procedure, at least state-
wide, so it varies from place to place.

I guess that I'm thinking that if there are no established rules then
you can reach out - maybe get the manager interested and informed
about the hobby - but then you also risk a strong adverse reaction to
something that could otherwise have happened quietly and without
trouble. I'm a supervisor and my staff may do some things that I
don't approve, but I'm not worried about that until I learn about
them - no time to go pulling everything apart looking for trouble.
