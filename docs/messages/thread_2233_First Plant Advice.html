I only have planted one, but I've watched a lot of posts like this, so
I can try to summarize them for you:

Plant really close to the trail, or very far away from the trail, or
somewhere in between.
Camouflage your box with tape or paint or a bag of some sort or just
rocks and sticks.
Make the clues and then plant, plant and then make the clues.
Post clues on AQ, LBMA, both, one of the other, or on your own website,
after all, it's special, it's your first box.

From: Otis' Friends | Date: 2007-01-04 02:04:35 UTC
We haven't done that much planting, but here are my cents.
 
1. Avoid "sensitive" areas (e.g., historic buildings, rock walls, delicate plants) and be respectful of the natural and human environments.
2. Don't plant too close to water.
3. Rocks are empirically proven to be good cover, especially if you ignore # 2.
4. Plant somewhere that is beautiful and/or interesting.  It should be a place people will be happy to have visited even if they don't find the box.
5. Don't plant in places that expose potential finders to personal danger and/or criminal/civil liability.
6. Camouflage is nice, but it's unclear that it actually makes a difference.
7. Make sure you mark on the box and/or log such warnings as "Not a Geocache," "Nothing leaves this box," and "Take the stamp and my cousin Jimmy will be paying you a visit."
8. As a bit of defensive driving, check the geocaching site for existing geocaches in the place where you are thinking of planting.  Best to know in advance if people will be stumbling across your letterbox as they use their spidey sense to narrow in on a geocache.  Geocachers often remove "prizes" placed by others and have been known to remove stamps by mistake.
9. Integration of site/clues/stamp is always appreciated - at least by me.  SSB is a master of this.
10. Use a decent box.  No Gladware.  Douggerware, Lock N Lock, and Posseware (those little army surplus decon containers) are the preferred choices of many.
11. Read Silent Doug's tips at his web site.
12. Have fun.  Actually, that should be #2.

Let me impart my lack of wisdom into this disscussion :-)

First off, don't sweat the details. I wouldn't worry too much about
things like story clues, puzzles, and so on. Just plant the box and
make it so folks can find it. The rest will come with time.

Don't worry about the stamp. If it is hand carved, don't worry that it
is not a work of art, no one expects it to be. Heck, if the location is
good, a store bought is just fine. Sheba and I found a box with just a
store bought the other day, but the location was great, and the hike was
nice as well.

Speaking of location, this is where you should really concentrate your
efforts. When choosing a location, take a look around. Is it close to
the trail? Does it look like it floods? Is there trash around?

You don't really want to be close to the trail, it is easier for Muggles
to spot that way. Look for plant that seem to all point in a single
direction, or soft muddy ground, these are clues to flooding. If there
is trash around, it may be a hang out for kids, and not a good place for
a box.

When planting, try to keep it a bit off the ground, it will stay drier
that way. Also, if it is on the ground, us rocks as cover, as they will
protect the box better. Rocks in a tree look suspicious, think natural
when planting the box.

Smaller is better, it is easier to hide. Bag well, double bagging the
log, but don't bag the outside, as these tend to get holes and pools of
water. You can put ink in it if you wish, but remember the Mid Atlantic
has about 30 freeze/thaw cycles in the winter, and that can devastate an
ink pad. Remeber seasons change, and so does plantlife. A box that is
easy to get to in the winter may be impossible in the summer, and a box
well hidden in the summer may be right in the open in the winter.

Have fun with it, just beware your surroundings.

From: thedoubtfulguests | Date: 2007-01-04 20:31:25 UTC
I just want to add:

- Should not be seen easily from the trail, with or without cover.
Avoid being in sight of "the nosey kid".

All the rocks, sticks, leaves, and camoflage you can think of will
not protect a box whose hidey hole faces a close trail or other
place where kids, or other muggles, are likely to play on or pass
by. Think like a nosey kid. I have seen too many holes in trees that
faced and were too close to the trail, or cracks in rocks that kids
would want to climb, etc. where the box was hidden but ain't there
no more.

From: nattybumppolbna | Date: 2007-01-05 14:01:53 UTC
I would like to echo the advice the esteemed Scarab and Cyclonic and just add that
keeping tabs on this group (and to a lesser extent the Newbies group - and even lesser
extent the national group) can help you be more sensitive to your audience while planning
your clue.

When I first started, I wasn't all that sensitive to the Finders wants and needs. I'm still not.
I'm NattyBumppo.

But I have an appreciation for the fact that a tough clue leading to a store bought stamp is
a big let-down for many. Also there are all types of 'boxers out there with differing
physical abilities. An enticing clue may be greatly disappointing when it is found to be
impassible to someone of more limited physical abilities. I'm not saying you should limit
your placements, just be kind and give people a heads-up.
Anyway, these things (and others) I have learned on this group. And while I do not always
follow them, I appreciate the game and the diversity of it's players more.

In conclusion, you can often learn a lot about good and bad boxes by reading a couple of
rants. But most importantly... just go do it! The real learning is in the doing. Hopefully
you will come back from the experience and teach us all something.
