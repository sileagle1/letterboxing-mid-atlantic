good letterbox containers

From: Silent Doug | Date: 2004-07-28 15:00:43 UTC
Like Scarab in his recent Andy Rooney "rant", which he allowed me to
reprint on my site at:
http://www.letterboxing.info/articles/00000025.shtml
I've been concerned about the various "substandard" containers in which
I've found being used in many letterboxes that I've found recently.

Having just replaced several letterboxes that were planted in 1999, I've
seen firsthand the damage that can be done by weather over the years. Maybe
it's unreasonable that letterboxes should be expected to survive for five
years, but I have also found boxes that had been planted 4 or 5 years ago
and were still holding up -- and a large reason why is the sturdy
containers that were used.

The "gladware" type boxes just don't hold up well out in the field, and
putting the whole letterbox inside a big plastic bag doesn't provide any
protection at all since they tend to rip or get holes quickly after being
pulled out from under rocks or logs just one or two times -- at which
point, they only serve to keep water close to the letterbox!

The best and least expensive boxes available on a regular basis that I've
found to date are available from Dollar Tree. They sell sturdy plastic
containers for $1 (everything's a dollar in the store, natch) called Milan
Classic, made by Holiday. There are two sizes, one roughly 5 1/2" square
(with a green lid) and one that's 7" square (with a blue or white lid);
they're deep enough to accommodate a stamp and log easily and the lids seal
securely (as evidenced by my "burp" test -- sealing the lid and then
pressing hard on it to see if air escapes. If air can get out, water will
get in -- try this on your potential letterbox containers and you'll
quickly see just how sturdy they are.). I started using them last year and
so far the ones that I've had out in the woods have stood up well through
the winter.

I've been stocking up on them, since they're a really great bargain,
especially compared to Rubbermaid boxes that are two or three times more
expensive and don't always hold up. And the price isn't that much more than
the disposal-type containers. I was happy to see that Celtic Cat &
Wednesday Bowler used one of the green lidded ones for their newest
letterbox, and would like to see better containers overall.

The other big reason that letterboxes survive and weather well is if the
logbooks are double bagged, preferably in sturdy "freezer-style" ziploc
bags that *don't* have the tab zippers. These are a bit harder to seal, but
you can burp out the air and check the seal when you replace the log in the
box to make sure that it is properly sealed. Every letterboxer should carry
some extra baggies in their letterboxing kit which can be used to replace
bags that get torn through normal use.

Just my two cents on letterbox containers!

Doug
