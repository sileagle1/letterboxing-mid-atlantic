From: stevelignowski | Date: 2007-05-18 17:25:31 UTC
I know it seems backward, but I had planned to do my first plant
before I'd ever even found a letterbox. I had the perfect subject, I
had written the story part, carved the stamp, printed out a first
finder certificate and put together a little prize. I had everything
I needed. Except, that is, a place to put it. I had read a story in
Weird NJ about Heartbeat Road, a road where there was a water pump
house that made a sound like a beating heart every five seconds or so
(way back when before there were houses in the vicinity). I googled
a satellite map and saw the perfect area to plant. I found out only
later that that area had since been developed and there was a horde
of condos there now. Don't believe a google map until you have
scouted the area in person. The area was just off Pavan Road in
Clifton, NJ. Here's the story I was going to put in my clue, just so
my work doesn't go to waste....:

She sat, arms folded, in her poodle skirt and saddle shoes,
staring straight ahead, looking bored as they drove along the back
road.
"Want to hear an interesting story?" he asked off-handedly.
She shrugged, unsure of what they were doing, or where they were
going. She'd never been down this road before; there were no
streetlights, only the headlights of the car and the stars overhead.
There was a wall of trees on either side. They were truly out
in the middle of nowhere.
"It's about this road that we're on. Some call it Heartbeat
Road. Many years ago, back when this area was first settled, there
were a boy and a girl, a lot like us, walking along this road. They
were set upon by a robber on horseback. They started to run. The
boy headed off into the woods, and didn't stop running until he was
quite a distance from the road. Minutes later, he heard the
hoofbeats as the rider disappeared. He made his way back to the
road, and quickly came upon the broken and bleeding body of his
girlfriend, palely visible in the moonlight. He rushed to her side
and took her in his arms, cursing his cowardice. He put his head to
her breast and heard the last feeble beats of her heart as it slowed,
and finally stopped. He then hung himself from a nearby tree."
She had goosebumps on her arms as he paused and stopped the car,
turning off the headlights and leaving them in darkness.
"They say," he continued, "that if you stop along this road and
listen, you can still hear the last beats of her heart."
He stopped talking. There was no sound around them but the
faint rustle of the wind through the trees. Then, in the distance..
Thump... thump.. thump.. thump.. thump..
She couldn't tell where the sound was coming from, but it was
definitely heartbeats, about five seconds apart.
The hairs on the back of her neck prickled. She had never been
more terrified in her young life. She grabbed at her boyfriend as
she stammered, "Ststart the car! Get us out of here!" She
continued to clutch at him holding her warm, trembling form tightly
against him.
Obediently, he turned the key and the car started. As they went
further along the road, the beating sound got louder, now audible
over the car's motor
THUMP... THUMP.. THUMP..
He could feel her nails digging into his skin, as she urged him
to go faster.
Finally, the sound faded into the distance, and they came back
to an area that was lit, and the comforting sounds of car traffic on
the highway could be heard.
He parked the car and put his arm around her still shaking
body. "Are you all right?" he asked.
She looked up at him incredulously, as he sat there calmly
stroking her hair, "Ddidn't you hear that sound back there?!"
He raised his eyebrows for a moment and replied, "Sure. That
was the old water pump house. What did you think it was?" He smiled
and kissed her on the forehead as she looked at him, stunned.
. . . . . . .

He grinned and chuckled to himself as he drove her home, his
shoulder still smarting from where she'd punched him.

Enjoy the brief tale, anyway. I may use the stamp in another box
later.
