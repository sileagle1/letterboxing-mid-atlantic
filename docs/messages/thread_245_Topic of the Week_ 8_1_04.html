Re: [lbma] Topic of the Week: 8/1/04

From: Hikers_n_ Hounds | Date: 2004-08-02 22:45:43 UTC
FdL,
 
I've given a lot of thought to this and realize I don't have a frame of reference on which to base my hypothesis (the current Mr. Irene is a scientist and he ALWAYS talks like this, even if I'm asking him if he thinks we need dog food LOL!). I can already see a difference since I started this past January, so I can't imagine a year or five years from now. I'll bet Silent Doug has seen a tremendous amount of change just in the last 2 years or so.
 
What I hope for are lot's of great boxes in neat places hidden by some really cool boxers. A good relationship with the PADNRC, or none at all (wink, wink, nudge, nudge say no more). To be allowed to plant and hike unfettered as long as we respect and care for the land we are enjoying. I keep thinking with horror of the mess down in GA and hope that never happens here. I would like the Mid-Altantic region state and county parks embrace letterboxingthe way many of the New England parks have. How can we make that happen? 
 
I think we are already on the way to a great community with lot's of funny, different and interesting people with a variety of likes and dislikes which should make boxing around here very interesting and diverse.
 
I appreciate Silent Doug and Jennifer's efforts to restore our early "historical" boxes and hope to search as many as possible so I can continue to understand and respect how much effort went into getting this hobby going in the US. It's just amazing when you think of what a handful of folks have accomplished in a few short years. I hope to never forget their efforts and am glad that someone is taking the time to keep the history of this hobby intact.
 
Wow, I feel like I am writing a campaign speech! "And to close, my fellow Americans...read my lips...no new taxes"! LOL!

Re: Re: Topic of the Week: 8/1/04

From: michael brooks | Date: 2004-08-13 02:51:41 UTC
Geocaching actually has more members than letterboxing in our region, and
probably every region outside of CT. For an example in my neighborhood, a
geocache in Carol Anne Farms Park in Virginia Beach has 100 finds logged,
while Lone Pony's letterboxes in the same park seem to have had at most 30
stamps (both have been placed long enough for everyone in the region in the
respective hobbies to find). In Fountainhead Park in northern Virginia, the
ratio between Shaggy (Creature of Fountainhead #5) and the geocache twenty
paces away was closer (at least when I found them last fall), but still with
more cachers.

Basic GPS units cost around $100 or so, which I think would put them in the
same magnitude as Game Boys and such. I don't think cost is a factor. Some
people prefer gadgetry while others prefer artistry. The outdoors should be
big enough for both.

The larger numbers mean that geocaching has had more scrutiny. They have
also responded to such scrutiny. In some areas, regional groups have formed
to work out arrangements and rules packages with park systems that were on
the fence as far as access. As a whole, geocaching at least as represented
by geocaching.com, obeys any regulations that are posted or announced by
land managers, like them or not. The theory is to cooperate with land
managers and lobby for favorable rules in the future. It makes more sense to
stay out of the hair of someone who doesn't want letterboxing and geocaching
around and hope they (or successors) change their minds than it does to have
repeated confrontations and try to wear an unfriendly park agency down. Park
agencies won't wear down in that manner. They'll remember groups and
activities that cause what they percieve to be problems.

While I haven't gone for the uber sized numbers that others persue, I have
been doing both geocaching (no GPS for me, I plot lat and long onto topo
maps) and letterboxing for over three years. Back when I started, there
weren't enough letterboxes nor geocaches individually to make a decent
hobby, so I started doing both and continue to do both. I fully believe that
no harm can come to any enviroment due to letterboxing or geocaching;
Scarab's "spiral search" observation of no impact ("Nature covers its
tracks") is relevant and I know from orienteering (it's been formally
studied) that even the biggest (15,000+ participants in Scandinavia) events
in that sport produce no lasting impact on the land either. We are
absolutely harmless to the enviroment and the problem is convincing the
troublesome land managers to acknowledge that.

I saw the notes for Delaware's problems. That is something that has just
happened in the last few weeks and the geocachers are getting hit too. There
is a small regional group called Delaware Geocaching through groups.msn.com
if any Delaware Letterboxers want to communicate with the them, maybe
coordinate your lobbying efforts. Since this is the first problem in
Delaware, they aren't as organized as cachers are in some other states. The
reason given so far is impact trails (bogus, there aren't enough cachers nor
letterboxers to cause those in Delaware either), but the suddenness of the
parks' change of heart suggests that some violation or offense (by cachers?,
boxers?, who knows?) occured somewhere. Delaware State Parks had been open
to both hobbies until the last few weeks.

I went ahead and joined LbSE since I have some letterboxes down there
anyway, so any further Georgia State Park thoughts will go there in a few
days. But that is one example of a regional caching group working with an
unfriendly park agency to try to turn them around. I suspect some of the
"silly rules" mentioned here refer to "locked boxes in the visitor center"
mentioned in the GSP policies. That's actually a compromise marshalled by a
Georgia geocacher. Obviously not ideal down there, but still a small
improvement over the "absolute NO" that the Georgia State Parks had been
giving. More on the other list in a few days.

As far as the original topic question, I think Scarab is right on the money.
I can't imagine there are any park agencies that don't know about
geocaching, and like it or not, letterboxing is going to be grouped with
them, since at the most basic level we are the same (hiding objects for
others to find). We are going to have to start transitioning to accept park
rules fully. One advantage is that the other guys (geocachers) have done
most of the work for us. Between geocachingpolicy.info and the regional
geocaching groups, we know what the rules are just about everywhere, with
Delaware SPs an exception, since they are in the midst of changing theirs.
And most of the trouble spots have groups that are working on solutions,
admittedly with different degrees of success. But letterboxing doesn't have
to start from scratch on any of these issues.

One final note, as if this wasn't long enough already. The first Mapsurfer
box (I'm not saying which one) that I sought and found is in a grossly
illegal spot. Since Mapsurfer boxes are prized, I went anyway. Spending 75%
of my time and effort in that location worrying about being caught by the
land manager greatly lessened the fun of that find. This is only a hobby.
It's not worth any kind of serious trouble. It might be disheartening to
know that "your favorite park" or a certain "really cool spot" doesn't
support this hobby, but there are plenty of outstanding parks that do. I
believe our hobby will be better off if we support the parks that support us
and ignore the ones that don't want us right now.

Happy letterboxing.

Mike
