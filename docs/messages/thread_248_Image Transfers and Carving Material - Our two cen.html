--- In newboxers@yahoogroups.com, Hikers_n_ Hounds
<hikers_n_hounds@y...> wrote:
Happy 5th of July All, hope your 4th was fun, happy and healthy.

There's always tons of posts on all of the lists about carving
material and images transfers. I save what sounds like good info and
review it with my son Carmen, who does all of our carving. He is
rarely on the trails with me but is still a member of the team. He
has tried the white stuff, the pink stuff, plumbers gasket, etc. He
said he can pretty much carve anything and likes the speedball pink
stuff the best but he said his trick is to carve all of the fine
detail FIRST, before you cut away large pieces of real estate. He
said that way the stamp surface is still firm and doesn't move or
wiggle when trying to carve delicate lines. When done this way you
can go back and touch and neaten things up because the surface is
still firm, but if detail is left to the end you must cut it right
the first time or run the risk of the material wiggling while cutting
and taking too much material away, destroying fine detail. So he does
all fine details first, then carves away the excess. And he
does it all with an A-type exacto knife, straight pin, speedball kit
and a magnifying glass. Someday, when I have the time to figure out
how to use our scanner, I'll post a file with our images. I think
he's pretty darn good, but remember, I am his Mom!

He has not tried the heat transfer method becuase he uses the pencil
method but does it perhaps a little differently than some. He tapes a
piece of tracing paper over his image, even if it's his original
artwork, which most of our stamps are. He then traces over it in
pencil. He goes very heavy on the fine detail lines, but only puts
lines or crosshatches on larger, solid areas. He says the trick is to
get the only as much lead and only on the areas you need to reduce
smudging. He then takes the tracing paper and puts it face down on
the carving material and uses a large paper clip to rub it and
transfer. Again, he's tried all kinds of stuff but says the large
rounded end of a paper clip works best for him because it applies a
lot of pressure per square inch but doesn't tear the tracing paper.He
goes over each individual line until the tracing paper seems to turn
white where the lines are and all lead has been transfered. After he
removes the tracing paper he then inks over top of
the lead and lets it dry a bit so it's resistant to smudging when
carving. That way if the lead smudges, he's still got a clean line to
follow. After that, he carves all of his fine detail first, then
finishes the stamp. We mount all of our stamps using rubber cement.
So far, so good.

So anyway, that's Hikers & Hounds 2 cents. Hope to see everyone at
the gathering. Carmen will be there so I am very excited.
