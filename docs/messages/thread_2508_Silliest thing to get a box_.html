Silliest thing to get a box!

From: Sara | Date: 2007-06-14 00:55:46 UTC
On Sunday we went boxing in the pine barrens.

While finding "Things to do in the pines" series, we ran into some
trouble... BUT THAT DIDNT STOP US. NOSIREE!

Box #1 - Attacked by 100s of ticks. Seriously.. every ten feet we
stopped and picked off at least 4 each.

Box #2 - More ticks, and some living creature guarding the box.. I
could hear him rustling!

Box #3 - Bridge out.. we waded across 20 feet of 6 inches of cedar
water! Man were our shoes wet.

Box #4 - No problem! Yay!

Box #5 - Heres the biggie.. the trail was flooded... REALLY FLOODED.
The pond took it over... I'm talking more than a foot and a half
deep, for about 500 feet. We went back to the car, got some
contractor grade garbage bags we had in the trunk and walked to the
box... potato sack style.. in our garbage bags.. through water up to
our knees right to the box. The box was on the only spot of dry land
around the pond! Yay box!

People camping across the lake saw us.. imagine what they were
thinking.

So .. that got me to thinking.. are other letterboxers as crazy as we
are when a box presents an unforseen obstacle?

TGD and I had a discussion about this. Being a dog owner and lover, I don't USUALLY get panicky when I see a dog running towards me. And he could never actually catch any of them. He loves poking around in the woods,sniffing out all of the different scents and exploring everything.  But Brian of TGD doesn't have a dog, and mentioned how HE deosn't know that a loose dog is friendly, even if the owner says so and especially if the owner is nowhere in sight just yet. While that is not always an indication of a calm and happy dog, in general in an aggressive dog, the tail will usually be erect and motionless.  The head will be very high, with ears pressed against the head.  The dogs stare is intense, direct and focused.  Teeth may be bared if the dog is growling. 
 
Anyway, I can appreciate how disconcerting it may be, especially with your little onces with you. I have been meaning to get mace or bear spray to keep at hand. Might be something to think about, unless you think the kids might actually get to it and spray you by mistake!

From: Brian, Ryan & Lori | Date: 2007-06-14 22:26:29 UTC
--- In lbma@yahoogroups.com, "4 suits" <lianedecker@...> wrote:

> Seriously, what is the proper response when you hear dogs running
> toward you on a trail?


Hey 4 suits

This happens more often than one would expect - especially on trails
that clearly state that ALL dogs must be leashed. I guess everybody
thinks that their dog is special or friendly or not a dog at all.

As soon as I am aware of a dog on the trail, by sound or sight,
leashed or unleashed, I stop. First I try to identify if an unleashed
dog is accompanied by an owner and if that is the case I ask the human
to secure their dog. Most pet owners will comply. If they do not, a
hint that the park authorities will be contacted will generally get
them to grudgingly put a leash on the dog while muttering something
about four flat tires in the parking lot. If an unleashed dog is not
immediately accompanied and I feel threatened by it, I try to remain
unnoticed by the animal and will sit down quietly (hopefully
unobserved) until the dog has moved far enough away that I can
continue on the path in the opposite direction of said pooch.

We have encountered unleashed, unattended, potentially threatening
dogs while on the trail with a child and the sitting quietly until the
dog went away worked for us. I think that this is something that you
can talk about with your children along the way and prepare them to be
quiet and try not to attract the dogs attention so that when the
situation arises, they will be more prepared to follow your "be quiet
and sit over here for a few minutes".

Don't look a strange dog in the eye.

Hope this helps as it seems that there really are a lot of unleashed
dogs out on the trails these days.

Lori of TGD (who grew up with dogs and is generally not afraid of them
but educated enough to understand their unpredictabilality)

Oh, dogs. Today was not a good day for me and dogs. I've never been afraid
of them before, but now I'm sure I'm going to jump a little if I see one
running toward me.

I got a summer job at a state park here in CT. We allow dogs here, on
leashes. A woman came in at about 8:07. I'll call her Early Lady. She had a
sweet smiling stocky dog in the back seat of her car. I took her cash and
said, "Oh, hi, puppy! Are you going for a walk today?" The dog got all
happy. Cute.

About 45 minutes later, a woman I'll call Crazy Lady came in. She told me
that she had four dogs in the back of her van, and that she was going to
mountainbike with them. I told her that dogs are welcome, as long as they're
on a leash. She told me she was going to take them two at a time, and she
needed to park in the shade, she was just going to park "over there." I
said, "Well, there's no parking right there." She drove over there anyway,
so I stepped out of my booth to tell her that she couldn't leave her van
there. She said that she would just park in the handicapped space, and I
said no, she wasn't handicapped.
As we were arguing (she was probably 10 feet away from me), Early Lady
approached me with her cute little dog on a leash.

She said, "What's that lady's problem?" I said, "She's just trying to figure
out where to park, I guess."

And then it happened so fast that it didn't register at first.

She said, "Oh, my God! Did my dog just bite you?"

I said, "No, I didn't feel--- OH MY GOD!" OK, it took a moment for the pain
to register. Holy explative. It hurt. It was a very hot red pain. It was
more like a burn than anything else.

Crazy Lady came running over and started chastising Early Lady for letting
her dog bite me. I went into my booth and shut the bottom half of the door.
My uniform shirt is very long, so I just pulled down my pants enough to see
it (right above my knee) and it was UGLY. And it hurt even more.

Early Lady put her dog in her car and flagged down the teenagers who work at
the park, who were driving around in the golf cart. They brought me an ice
pack and I called (my supervisor) Mr. Ranger, who was at another park. Mr.
Ranger said that he couldn't tell me what to do, but he advised me to get to
a doctor. Early Lady drove me, even though I said that I could drive myself.

They gave me a tetanus shot and I got to take a good look at the bite. It's
a diagonal VERY swollen area of about six inches from the top puncture to
the bottom. Right now it's all kinds of pretty colors. I might post a photo
if anyone wants to be grossed out. ;) He put some peroxide and bacitracin
(sp?) on it and bandaged me up and gave me a prescription for antibiotics.
One of the DEP (Department of Environmental Protection... they run the parks
and forests) cops (I bet you didn't know they have their own police force)
came to check on me and take my statement.

Early Lady drove me to the pharmacy to fill the prescription, paid my $3
copay and brought me back to the park.

After I got back, I called Mr. Ranger, who came by to take his own
statement.
I'm going to call Early Lady's vet in a few (I have all her information) to
find out if the dog is really up to date on rabies vaccinations. I didn't
mind the tetanus shot, but the rabies shots are going to be an explitive if
I need them.

She told me that she may have the dog put down over this, and I got really
upset. I mean, doesn't every dog bite someone once in a while? No? I don't
know.
I've never owned one.

Anyway, yeah. Animal Control is going to call her and probably quarantine
her dog for 10 days. I'm going to soak my wound for 15 minutes a day and
take antibiotics for 10 days.

She told me that the dog had never done this before, but that she's feeling
old and out of sorts and stressed, and maybe that's why? I don't know. I
just can't believe it happened. OW. Owie, owie, ow.

Don't tell your daughter this story. ;)
