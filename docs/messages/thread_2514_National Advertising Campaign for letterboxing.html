National Advertising Campaign for letterboxing

From: Chris Bamberger | Date: 2007-06-16 12:49:41 UTC

The July/August issue of Family Fun appeared in my mailbox yesterday.  Imagine my surprise as I flipped through it and it fell open to an advertising insert titled ‘Welcome to Letterbox Camp’ sponsored by the Chevy Suburban.  The page is rather well done.  It provides a good explanation of letterboxing with reference to letterboxing.org and atlasquest.com (I hope Chevy gave them money for using their URL in an advertisement).  They do say to be discrete and hide the box better than you found it.  There is a lexicon, giving basic definitions for things like ‘trail name’ and ‘hitchhiker’.  They even include instructions on how to carve your own stamp with a pattern for a (surprise!) Suburban.  They reference a website for more – www.familyfun.com/letterboxcamp - but that doesn’t seem to be active. 

 

I’m stunned.

 

 

Chris

From: Joyce | Date: 2007-06-20 14:02:04 UTC
Your response made me do alot of research (most of the following is
taken straight from their website unless indicated by IMO)...

Yes, Geocaching's website is actually run by Grondspeak which is a
company with paid employees and over 100 volunteers. The three
head honchos were the ones who placed/found some of the first boxes,
did the first website, and/or came up with the geocaching name.

With Jeep, they release 5000 Jeep travel bugs each year. Once you
find one, you register and your name gets entered to win a Jeep.
They also have "diabetes" travel bugs and a bunch of other "travel
bugs" from misc events, causes, etc. Anyone can develop a bug.

So yes, with Geocaching, there is Groundspeak sanctioned
advertising that they get paid for. The website has advertsing. So
when the Jeep bugs get released in August, the advertising will be
everywhere. On the website, in magazines, etc.

BUT, Geocaching also has over 416000 caches worldwide (from 0 in
April 2000). In the last week, there were 279617 logs by 42013 log
holders. And when you look at numbers like that, IMO, Geocaching
is totally out of the bag and they need the rules and structure to
be able to handle that level of interest.

IMO, I think that there are pluses and minues to each group. But
the fact that letterboxing touts itself as unorganized is why
letterboxing can be "taken advantage" of in this way. And I don't
mean that to upset anyone, but it is one of the downsides of being
a "hobby" where you try to keep it like it always has been.

Joyce
