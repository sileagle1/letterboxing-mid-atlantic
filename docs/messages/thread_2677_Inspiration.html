Re: [lbma] Inspiration

From: Otis' Friends | Date: 2007-09-12 02:13:34 UTC
One method:  Think of a stamp you're interested in, then think of a logical place where that person/place/thing "belongs."  (Or, think of a great place to put a box and carve a stamp that has something to do with the history or geography of that place.)  Then think up a story about your stamp.  As you picture the story in your mind, match plot points up to landmarks along the way to the stamp.
 
That Games magazine may also help.  Even if you don't use the same puzzles, it helps to start thinking about other ways to create puzzles.
 
If there's another hobby you're involved in, think about whether it has recognized conventions that might be useful for building a clue - something that isn't readily obvious to everyone, but that becomes clear once you realize what the clue is all about.
 
The most important thing to do is to trust your instincts.  If it seems to you like something that works, it does. 
 
If you hit a wall, take a break.  Don't surround yourself with yourself.  Move on back two squares.

From: Lightnin Bug | Date: 2007-09-12 11:08:19 UTC
Otis is dead on correct. Another thing is to ponder the clue concept
or structure first, then create the stamp to match that. Some ideas
just pop into your head when cutting the lawn or some such. The box
location becomes secondary as far as that is concerned, just that it
be appropriate for hiding a mystery box due to the theme of the clue
or in a spot that is unusual or scenic for whatever reason.

In the end, there are many ways to make things mysterious. From
simple substitution ciphers to the field of semiotics (look it up on
Wikipedia), something that good old mapsurfer employs in most of his
great eevil works.

Cheers,

LB

From: brendan_hickey13 | Date: 2007-09-12 15:03:09 UTC
Agreed with Otis' Friends and Lightnin Bug.

To add a bit, I think that the answer to this question is really to
listen to yourself - what interests you? - and then to tune into
those ideas or activities or places or whatever. We've based boxes
on a cool local ghost story, a former WWII army base, a park in my
hometown, some beers that we like, etc. Sometimes the inspiration is
what annoys you - whatever stirs a reaction can be the basis for a
box, and in that is the some of the artistry in letterboxing.

I think that the box and clues and location should share a context.
If you want me to walk a few miles for your box, that's cool - but
don't have me take 5 laps around a borough park - instead, put the
box in large park and give me a tour. Better yet, take me to your
favorite part of the park and plant a stamp that represents it.

That's the inspirational part but, being said, be silly if you want
to be silly.

The great thing about this game is you can do whatever works for you. Want to show
somebody a nice hike? Doesn't matter what your box, stamp or clue is. They will enjoy
the hike. I have series that I have designed based on location. I have boxes that I have
designed based on clues and puzzles. Mostly, my work takes me to interesting places all
over the country. I like to leave boxes behind. So I find a place to hide and try to work up

planning.
