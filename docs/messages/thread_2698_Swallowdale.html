From: Otis' Friends | Date: 2007-09-26 12:54:23 UTC
If they aren't adopted but are still in the wild, if she has retired those boxes will rely on the kindness of strangers for maintenance.  If they do not receive it, they will become litterboxes.
 
If they aren't adopted and are not still in the wild, the clues will remain available through the internets.  This can create problems if people tear the place apart in frustration looking for something that isn't there.  They won't just die on their own.
 
If they are adopted, the adoptive parent will presumably provide maintenance.  That person can also move the box or change the clues if that's appropriate, or even retire the box altogether.  Atlas Quest throws all clues open to adoption after the placer fails to log in for a year.  (Side note:  That should probably be disclosed to people when they post clues.  I can't remember ever seeing anything about it on the clue screens.)  LBNA is a little less transparent, but I gather they ping the placer after a certain time and move things to the basement if there is no response.
 
As we've seen from the unwarranted attacks on Rick for his Good Samaritan efforts to rehab apparently abandoned boxes, adoption can be a messy process.   For it to have legitimacy and to prevent unnecessary rifts from developing, a few principles should be followed.  The original placer needs to have notice and an opportunity to be heard.  And it should probably be a reversible thing.  Those same principles should apply whether we're talking about Mr. P50F5000X250 or Greenhorn McNewby.
 
As for whether or not Swallowdale eats babies, which I sincerely doubt, I'm withholding comment on that for a few days.
