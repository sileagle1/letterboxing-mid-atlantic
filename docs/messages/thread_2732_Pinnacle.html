Re: Pinnacle

From: Lightnin Bug | Date: 2007-10-09 12:22:03 UTC
The Pinnacle is within State Game Lands, so Sunday hikes in the fall
is recommended, lest ye wear Blaze Orange. Even state laws will not
prevent the hardened poacher, but you are less likely to run into one
on Sunday.

I would give the entire circuit about 5-6 hours (if including Pulpit
Rock). It is a 8-9 mile round trip, has 1000 ft elevation gain and
when on the ridge line, is very rocky (makes French Creek look
paved). Bring a lunch and if it is warm, bring a LOT of water. I
recommend a couple quarts at least. It is a wonderful hike, for thos
eso inclined (yes, I am a hiking masochist in the eyes of some).

Cold front is coming this week. Will it be cold enough to drive those
venomous biters underground, I am not sure.

Hoep this helps,

LB

Re: Pinnacle

From: Lightnin Bug | Date: 2007-10-09 13:07:42 UTC
Unlike the rocky areas of Pohopocotepetl, the rocks between Pulpit
Rock and the Pinnacle and in the last 1/2 mile to the Pinnacle along
the ridge line are all fixed in place, though just as numerous. It
should be an easier hike in terms of comparing rocky with loose
rocky. There is the larger elevation gain, though.

There even is an impromptu monument in the vicinity of the Pinnacle
where people have placed rocks that they have gathered in an effort
to appease the gods of PA Rocky Trails (hopeign that the rocks will
be smitten and paved over). It is a pile of rocks some 15 ft high
that hikers keep adding to. Well, it was 15 ft high in 2004,and it
may be bigger now. Talk about your SPOR.

Pinnacle Report

From: Otis' Friends | Date: 2007-10-15 03:05:19 UTC
Total time was a bit high, but a lot of that was spent picnicking and watching hawks, among other things.  Made it up to Pulpit Rock in about an hour, only to discover I had forgotten to put my compass back in the gear.  That was too much of a handicap for me to find the Pulpit Rock boxes, but they could very well be there.  Spent about an hour at Pulpit Rock (including work calls over by the cell phone tower), then set out for the Pinnacle.  Took about an hour and a half to get there.  It lives up to the rep and I hope I can post photos soon.  Fortunately, I was able to find the Pinnacle letterbox sans compass.  It was in good shape, with quite a few finders in the last couple of years.  I love finding the ones that are several years old.  Saw probably close to a hundred people at the Pinnacle and on the trail, including a few large Mennonite families (also with babies on board). Spent about an hour or more at the Pinnacle, then took about two hours to get back to Pulpit Rock.  Took a last stab at the boxes there and headed down.  All said, I'd say walking time was about 5 - 6 hours but total time was considerably more.

Re: Pinnacle Report

From: Lightnin Bug | Date: 2007-10-15 12:16:21 UTC
Excellent report! One of my favorite Pinnacle tales I like to tell
involves a large group of Mennonites. We were up there one day on a
particularly clear August day (A cold front had come through a day or
so earlier) and one of the gentlemen and I were talking about how
clear everything was (you could see the mountains in NJ about 50
miles east - they were blue that day). He pointed to a small shiny
spot across the great valley to an area south of Kutztown on the
upslope of the Reading priong (FYI - South Mountain in Allentown and
Mt. Penn in Reading) and said "You see that shiny spot? That is my
barn. I got a new roof made out of aluminum and I can see if from
all the way up here!" My guess is that the barn was about 12 miles
away, but it was very cool to see that the gentleman was vey proud of
it.

Happy trails,

LB
