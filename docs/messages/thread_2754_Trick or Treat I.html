Trick or Treat I

From: nattybumppolbna | Date: 2007-10-20 12:31:17 UTC
It was common in urban graveyards in days past, when the cemetary ran out of space, to dig
up the older family plots and consolidate the bones, making room for new family members.
Despite the grisly work, propriety was always observed. Families were buried together,
husbands and wives and children with grandparents and great-grandparents.

And then came the modern era. Churches, under economic stress would sell off property to
fund repairs to aging buildings. Or modern inventions, like the automobile, would require
that land be re-purposed for things like parking lots. Often, churchyard graves would need
to be moved. Sometimes they would be moved and reburied without regard. Coffins would
be moved en masse and reinterred as a group, familes separated for eternity. The older
graves, really just piles of bones now, mixed up and buried in a pile; some here, some there.

Now if a spirit was still tied to this world through some accident or oversight or madness,
imagine their dismay at having their last mortal remains treated so casually. Imagine the
wrath of an angry spirit when its power is at its peak on Halloween night.

Trick or Treat II

From: nattybumppolbna | Date: 2007-10-22 00:11:18 UTC
In this era of everyday evil - evil that is so commonplace as to be mundane - it is difficult to
gauge those things of days past which could make a soul bond itself to a location for eternity
and forego paradise. When children can take up arms to slaughter their classmates with the
ease of battle-hardened vets, it is hard for the modern mind to imagine the spirit of a little
girl clinging to this earth because of a lost item like a toy or mirror. And yet, there are often
simple - not horrific - reasons behind many of our local haunts.

When one sees white-clothed men and women walking through suburban woods, when one
hears screams, we are tempted to look for a grisly reason for the haunting: a death by
disease or murder or suicide. There are stories, for example, of a suicide in the nearby barn,
now an art center, but there are no records - no indication of anything of the kind happening
there. Sometimes when confronted with the supernatural, people seek a reason and finding
no reason, make one up.

All we know for sure is that there have been multiple, independent sightings of unnatural
beings walking through the woods, and screams with no apparent origin. Beyond that, we
know nothing. But then, if you see ghostly figures walking through the woods at night and
hear their screams, do you really need a reason to be afraid? Very, very afraid...?

Trick or Treat III

From: nattybumppolbna | Date: 2007-10-22 18:31:55 UTC
In 1957 Timothy McIlvain was an 8 year-old boy living just outside Philadelphia. He didn't
go to school. Deaf children didn't go to public school in those days and most people
could not afford private schools or tutors.

Like many kids in the days before "stranger danger," Timothy would leave home in the
morning and explore his world all day long. He would play in the creek. He would walk
through the woods. He would also play on the railroad tracks.

Of course, Timmy never heard the train coming when it struck him from behind and rolled
over him, severing his legs and his head from his body. His head, it is believed, must have
rolled into the drainage ditch and washed away. It was weeks before the rest of his body
was discovered and months before it was identified.

The area changed. Houses were built. A typical suburban neighborhood grew. In the
1970's the railroad tracks were removed leaving the raised bed and the path behind the
houses. But the train still runs. Residents along the line still awaken to the sound of the
whistle and the train still shakes the houses a bit as it passes. Children who grew up in
the area have all played with someone who has played with the little deaf boy who often
hangs out where the railroad used to run.

In the 1980's tragedy struck the quiet neighborhood when a local boy committed suicide
in his garage. That boy was last seen playing basketball at a net on the street by the
entrance to the old railroad track path. He was playing with a younger boy who looked out
of place in his old fashioned clothes.

Re: Trick or Treat IV

From: nattybumppolbna | Date: 2007-10-27 00:14:56 UTC
In centuries past, the mentally ill were believed to be possessed by demons. They were
treated in the only way available: exorcism. Is it possible that today we believe the
possessed are mentally ill and treat them with drugs and therapy? Is mental illness and
satanic possession the same thing?

Imagine the plight of the mentally ill in the 19th century. Convinced by hundreds of years
of custom and tradition that they are possessed by demons, but denied the release of
exorcism by the new science of psychology. Confined in asylums in brutal living
conditions, subjected to experimental "treatments," the insane or possessed are left to die
slow quiet deaths in isolation when the treatments fail.

Imagine being convinced that you are possessed by a demon. What does the afterlife hold
for you? An eternity of torture at the deepest level of Hell? Convinced of this fact, what
reason would your spirit have to leave this world when death finally comes? Given the
horrific conditions of asylum life, could some souls believe that already inhabit the
Netherworld?

Most of the area people know about building 17. Stories have been passed down through
generations about the things that went on inside its walls. But the worst stories, the most
chilling tales are the stories of things that have occurred since the building was closed
down and boarded up. Because, as many who have unadvisedly explored inside will tell
you, it has not been completely abandoned. There are still things inside. Things that may
be just looking for some company.

Trick or Treat V

From: nattybumppolbna | Date: 2007-10-28 02:48:37 UTC
In 1778 Christian Funk was excommunicated from the Mennonite church. His crime was
speaking out in support of the new nation and the revolution. He and his followers formed a
new church. Some of them even fought in the war despite the Mennonite belief in pacifism
and non-violence.

Forced to choose between God and country, they took up arms in the new nation's defense.
The church died out in the 1850's and very little is known about them. But if you go to one
of the few remaining cemeteries on a moonless night, it is said you can see the followers of
Christian Funk standing by their graves waiting
