It isn't often I get to attend a gather, what with 6 days a week mail delivery.  I was especially sorry to be missing this haunting---until I came across the following in the Dead Letter archives.  Postmarked from Mount Savage MD 1907 the envelope had obviously gotten wet as the ink for the addressee had become illegible.

The contents appears to be a newspaper article, but no indication of who it was intended for or who sent it.  Anyone staying at The Castle in Mount Savage, or even meeting there, should take care and at the very least do not venture out alone.  Here is what was written.
*******************

Jack the Ripper is the popular name of the unknown killer who terrorized London between August and November of 1888. (He was also called the Whitechapel Murderer, after the city district where he operated.) The exact details of the case are uncertain: five women are generally considered to be definite victims of the Ripper, though there may have been more or less. All were strangled and then had their throats cut, and many were further mutilated with a "surgeon's skill".

The nickname Jack the Ripper came from the signature on a letter sent to a news agency during the rampage. Just when the London Police thought they were closing in on the identity and close to an arrest. The killings stopped as abruptly as they began. London police were unable to solve the case or find a firm suspect. The case was closed officially in 1892, but the mysterious anonymity of the killer has kept the case in the public eye ever since. Some close to the investigation say the Whitechapel Murderer relocated to America where he may have had business interests and opportunity to return to the shadows.

In December of 1888 Alexander Thompson accepted the position of as company doctor for the British owned Mt Savage Enameled Brick Company. Dr. Thompson bought "The Castle" overlooking Mt Savage and made it his home for the next nineteen years.

In January of 1889 a series of mysterious and vicious murders began to occur in the otherwise quiet villages of Western Maryland . Mt Savage, Frostburg, Cumberland and just across the Pennsylvania border at Wellersburg Pennsylvania . The details of these heinous crimes were not released but victims were found at the Mt Savage iron works, clay and coal mines, brick-works, railroad tracks and even near Old Row. The local newspapers nicknamed the killer "The Savage Fiend" after the area where he seemed to be operating. The publicity seemed to cause the murders to cease in Mt Savage and increase in the other towns and villages, but the Savage Fiend nickname stuck.

The killings were sporadic and there were no clues to the killer's identity until eighteen years passed. While patrolling the brick works, a night watchman came face to face with the Fiend himself. Darkness and a face covered in blood prevented positive identification. The Fiend was found crouching over his latest victim still clutching a bloody knife. The Fiend was startled and immediately fled but the watchman gave chase and shouted for help. He chased the Fiend thru the village banging on doors and windows along the way to sound the alarm; "It's the fiend it's the fiend!" he called out as he ran up the hill to the Castle walls where he lost the trail. The fiend seemed to have vanished in thin air at the Castle owned by Dr. Thompson.

Dr. Thompson immediately became a prime suspect. But he was never again seen.  When police arrived they found a note saying he had returned to England   "on business " and was never seen again. The local newspapers had a field day and all but convicted him in their pages. In all, there were 33 unsolved murders in the Western Maryland Villages between 1889-1907.

Unable to contact Dr. Thompson, the Castle was eventually seized for back taxes and auctioned off by the town council. Found among Doctor Thompson's possessions were many surgical blades. Police were shocked to find many cards, letters and photographs of Whitechapel.

News of the auction and amazing Whitechapel connection were widely publicized. October 27 1907 the local newspaper received an ominous letter written in Dr. Thompson's hand, cursing the town for the loss of his property and vowed; " that he if it took 100 years he would he would return to Mt Savage and to take back what was his!" It was mockingly signed  "the Savage Fiend".

 *******************
