Re: [lbma] Carving Tools

From: MaryAnn Lockard | Date: 2007-12-30 12:50:12 UTC
Most of us work with just those and get excellent
detail. One way to get an even finer blade on the
speed ball #1 gouge is to heat it in a flame then
insert an exacto blade in the "valley" take a pair of
pliers and squeeze the edges closes together.
One other carving tool I have is a carving needle. It
is in fact about an 18 gauge needle in a pin vice. It
took me awhile to figure out how to use it. It is used
like an exacto blade. I really don't use it much.

I agree with Mizscarlet. I do almost all of my carving with the smallest speedball tool and get excellent results and can get some really fine detail. It takes a lot of practice. The tools mentioned are pretty much all there are on the market as far as I know. The amount of detail you get get easily also depends on the carving meduim you use. I also use a magnifying lamp. That makes a huge difference with detail.

I use all Exacto knives, the big rubberized handle with #11 blades. It
is what I learned on and works well for me. I have seen amazing stamps
using either Exactos or carving gouges, or a combination of either, so I
tend to think that choice of tools does not really matter so much.

There are some material choices that are better then others. You will
want to try and go with the Speedball Speedy Stamp (the pink stuff),
Master Carve or PZ Cut. I prefer the PZ cut as it is a bit harder and
does not seem to move around much when you are working detail. Master
Carve is soft and very easy to work with. Some people freeze it as they
are working with it to make it a bit harder. Speedy Stamp seems to be
the most readily available, AC Moore seems to be carrying it now
locally, though with have a local art store that also carries it and I
prefer to buy it there (so do others it seems, as they were sold out
last week).

I have found that it is not so much what tool you use, or even the
material you use, but rather the time, patience and practice you put
into it. With all materials you should try and work the detailed parts
first, and work the larger areas afterwards. This will keep the material
solid as you work those tiny areas.

Take your time. I have spent many hours on just a single stamp, as have
others.

Have a good, clean, well lit work area with everything you need at hand.
Or at least make sure it is well lit, as is usually my case ;-)

It does not all come over night, but it will come, and nearly everyone
can carve to some degree given the practice. Do not be discouraged by
failed attempts, failure is a good thing as it helps you learn and
improve. Don't be afraid to ask for help, go to a gathering and ask,
there are always folks willing to give and impromptu lesson.

Most of all, have fun. Carve what you enjoy to carve, when you want to.
Take a break when you need one. It is no fun when it becomes a chore.

Good luck, I look forward to finding some of your boxes.
