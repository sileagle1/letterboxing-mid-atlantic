Re: [lbma] A Morality Question

From: Katrina Lustofin | Date: 2008-02-06 00:36:24 UTC
I agree with mizscarlet - it is one thing to get hints from the group.  It's one of the advantages of having boxing buddies - you can compliment each other's skills.  I have one boxing buddy who cannot read a compass to save her life, but I have lost count of the number of times she's picked out a landmark that I missed. 

I also don't have a problem with people asking me as the placer of a box for help.  I'm happy to give clues or hints if the boxer has put effort into it.  But the time that another boxer posted geocache coordinates for a quasi-mystery box of mine really upset me.  I don't think it is at all acceptable to broadcast hints;  one of the nice things about letterboxing is that there are boxes at all difficulty levels - both in the physical hike and the mental challenge.   Not all boxes are meant to be found by lots of people, and I'm okay with that, both as a placer and a finder.  Heck, Kimono Girl and I just made plans to celebrate my visit to the Midwest and our upcoming 4th anniversary of letterboxing by trying once again to find a box for which we've looked 4 times now.  Eventually, we will find it.  In the meantime, it's the one that got away, the big fish, the box we talk about all the time - the box we recruited her husband, at that time a non-boxer, to help us find, the box that turned him into one of the busiest planters in Urbana for a while.  We'll find it someday.  And if we don't, well, it's been great fun trying.  And fun is why we got into letterboxing in the first place.

Katy

From: brendan_hickey13 | Date: 2008-02-07 15:19:54 UTC
My view is that only the owner can declare a box MIA because the owner
knows where s/he placed the box. It's a big leap from "I can't find
it" to "It's gone" - not the same thing at all.

Syner and I have found box bits and reported that to the placer, but
even with the evidence of a muggled box in hand, it wasn't for us to
call the box gone.

If an MIA report suggests that the attempt was off then the placer
should probably say so, but otherwise, the placer might take the
opportunity to check on the box or enlist someone familiar with the
box to check it.

From: Katrina Lustofin | Date: 2008-02-07 16:59:24 UTC
I concur with brendan.  One of my boxes has been declared MIA several times by boxers but is always there when I check on it.  The clues are a little tricky because you can start in the wrong place and find landmarks that exactly match - but then you are looking for the box more than a mile away from where it is planted.

From: wood thrush | Date: 2008-02-07 19:25:28 UTC
For me it all boils down to whether the planter is active or not. My
own practice is that I never ask about boxes where I know the planter
is currently active. So for example, I would never ask Natty about
his boxes, nor ask anyone else about them in the online boards.

I do feel differently about older boxes with checkered histories
where the planters are no longer active. So, for example in the last
few days here there was a small discussion about the Mobots and the
Hidden Harvest box. That, I feel, is kosher, especially since for
those of us local to this box, we have some idea of the situation. In
addition, MB, who brought up the topic, is a very seasoned and
dedicated finder in these parts who has rendered numerous first aid
and other kindnesses to boxes for several years. A good boxer, and
one who has solved many a mystery box to boot. I know that if MB is
moved to ask about a box, there must be a real reason for it.

I also think there are a few circumstances when it is OK to
correspond offline with folks who post an online question about a box
I've previously found---again, if the placer is no longer active. One
is if the box is not only missing, with clues still online, but also
planted in a very dangerous place. There is one box in our area like
this, and whenever I see online that someone is searching for it, I
email them offline and mention that it is not only missing, but
originally had been planted in a dangerously decrepit area known for
drug dealing.

The other time I correspond offline is for an orphaned box I have
found that is still active but long ago had been moved to a different
location. The clues were never updated, the planter is long gone, and
boxers keep looking in the original place when the poor box is just a
little further down the trail.

As the saying goes, your mileage may vary. But this is what I've come
up with for myseslf.

That's a good observation. I would say this particular place
looks "iffy" from the start, but at least to my eyes it was a "let's
see how this goes" kind of iffy.

Unfortunately, the several times I have made that kind of call, I
have come away from the experience (whether or not I have found the
box) thinking to myself, "you know, that was not a very smart thing
for me to do." This particular one was just the clearest example of
that, especially after actually coming across drug dealers with
multiple cops hot on their trail while I was there.

I think you run into these kinds of judgment calls more in urban
and "close-in" suburban boxing and in boxing where you do not know
the area very well, such as in vacation boxing.

If I am a local and I know the planter is no longer involved in
boxing, and the box is missing and/or I know there's a potential
danger to finders... well, if I see that box mentioned online, I feel
it is a service to other boxers to inform them offline about what I

I recently witnessed an occurence where one boxer was asking a
planter for a clue to one of their mystery boxes. It seemed obvious
to me that the planter did not want to offer any additional info.
The boxer that was asking certainly seemed like a nice person and
well intentioned in their desire to seek the box...but it took a
couple of friendly exchanges back in forth before the boxer stopped
asking for additional hints.

I myself have asked for a hint on occassion. I recently asked for a
hint to a box that I had searched for 2 times without luck. It turns
out that after I described the details to the placer....and they went
and checked on it.....that the box was actually not there. I could
have asked for info sooner....such as after the first attempt, but I
like putting forth the effort in looking for the box. I also wanted
to be sure that I was indeed checking the right spot....as the clues
were pretty tough to figure out...and besides that...I really liked
the area where the box had been placed. A great 3 mile hike - each
way.

I see nothing wrong with asking for some info from a placer. Some
boxers will put forth alot of effort to find a box first...while
others will merely look at a clue and ask for help in solving it.
Regardless of the circumstance, it is certainly the planter's choice
to decide whether they want to offer help and at what point that help
is deemed to be warranted. If a boxer is not happy with the response
from the placer...then they should go look for another box. I
myself, like many of you, have come across some boxers who don't
bother to respond to e-mails...and that's the way life is.

I have found over 200 boxes and still have yet to plant one. I have
several locations all mapped out and the clues are ready to go...but
as of yet...I still do not like my carvings. So the boxes will wait
for now....but not for too much longer. And will I offer hints to my
boxes?? It depends. If the effort was there and I am presented with
sufficient info that shows you were nearby...I may throw a hint your
way. But then again.........maybe not. LOL. -John
