"Life's Little Adventure", A Letterboxing Story

Today, WhisperFoot, Baby-Boo, and I found the "Life's Little
Adventure" letterbox alongside the Loch Raven Reservoir in
Cockeysville, Maryland. Not a major accomplishment typically worth
writing about: the box itself was in good shape, it was an easy and
short hike, the logbook wasn't moldy, stamp was store bought, plenty
of boxers have stamped in, less than 15mins round trip from parking to
stamping and back on the road. Quick, easy, standard fare - but the
name of the box and the entirety of the story might be interesting to
others infected with the Letterboxing parasite.

So, today was actually our second attempt at finding this box. Our
first attempt was Thanksgiving Day '06. Thanksgiving Day, you ask?
Yes, we try to get outdoors to hike and box on every opportunity
including holidays. The plan was to dash out grab a few boxes, dash
home, and join the family for Turkey-day dinner. We had chosen a
couple of drive-by/short-hike boxes in Baltimore County Maryland
specifically because it was a nasty weather day (raining, low-40's; in
my opinion-probably the worst weather, period) and because WhisperFoot
had just given birth to Baby-Boo a couple of weeks earlier and was
nursing. After a relatively short drive and finding another drive-by
box we arrived at Life's Little Adventure. The rain hasn't let up and
had, in fact, intensified. Upon arriving Baby-Boo woke-up and started
demanding lunch, as newborns do. Putting on our multi-tasking hats
was a no-brainer; WhisperFoot & Baby-Boo would stay behind nice and
dry in the car, take care of feeding while I would head out into the
downpour, find the box, stamp us in, and return. Nothing
earthshattering with this plan except, as Robert Burns so aptly stated
"The best laid plans of mice and men often go awry."

So, I suited-up and headed off down the trail to find the box.
Whisperfoot and Baby-Boo stayed behind in the car, which is parked at
the bottom of a gully situated between several large hills overlooking
the Reservoir. The trail heads uphill and the Box's clue states that
I should go "about 3/4 up the trail". So I start heading uphill. It
is fairly hard to describe how steep this hill is and/or how long the
trail is, but this is a substantial grade following an, obviously
rarely-used, fire-road into the hills around the Reservoir. Being
that I follow directions rather literally, I look up the hill and
calculate that "3/4 up the trail" is 3/4 up the hill and it is going
to take me about 10-15mins strenuous hiking through several
switchbacks until I should start looking for the next part of the
clue-- which is a small side trail. This is not really the
drive-by/short-hike that we had in mind, but I am already wet and I
have like 15-20 minutes to kill anyway as Baby-Boo is feeding. So,
I'm in pretty good shape, I go for it. I climb and climb and climb.
At every switchback I look uphill to calculate how much closer to 3/4
up the hill I am, and I do keep an eye out for the side trail -- clues
are often fubar'd and backwards, right? Climb, climb, climb, and the
trail never ends, and no side-trail in sight. 10 minutes turns into
15, into 20; pass through switchback #3, 4, 5, hey, now I can see the
top of the hill and the houses that line the hilltop --they have a
really nice view!-- but this damn trail keeps on going. I am now out
of breath, and i realize that I have another 20mins to get back to the
car and that I must have *missed* the side-trail (it is raining and I
am only human, right?). So I turn around and head back down the hill
- keeping an eye out for that side trail. Down, down, down through
all the switchbacks. No side trail. Finally back to the parking lot
and the car. Okay, I think, I must be blind and stupid, but its
raining and its a holiday so I will just email the planter and ask for
better clues.

I get back to the car, totally soaked to the bone all the way through
my socks, where WhisperFoot gives me the "so this was a drive-by?"
look; Baby-Boo had finished feeding 20mins ago and is about to fall
back to sleep. I explain what happened and recommend that we punt on
the rest of the day, go home, and just enjoy the rest of the holiday.
WhisperFoot agrees and goes to start the car. She turns the key and
gets the dreaded -click--click--click- of a dead car battery. While I
was off hunting the box we had accidentally left the car lights on.
Oh shit- the ~45mins I was gone the freaking lights had been on and
drained the battery. Now what, we are basically in the middle of
nowhere. Okay.... We are AAA members in good standing, right? We did
pay the membership last summer, right? We will just call them up and
get jumpstart. No big deal, happens all the time. Okay, we dig out
the membership card and fire-up the cell phone. Whats this? No
signal? wtf? Oh, we are in a gully at the bottom of a reservoir.
But we are only 3miles from York Road, one of the busiest roads north
of Baltimore. Doesn't matter, we are in the bottom of a gully and
physics don't lie. There is no cell signal. We are stuck.

So honey, you have been sitting here for the last 45mins, how many
cars have gone by; can we flag down a passing motorist? Whats that-
maybe 2-3 cars in 45mins? Oh yeah, its Thanksgiving Day and we are
really off the beaten path... double-shit. Okay, now what. I guess I
have to hump it up the hill until there is enough signal to get
through to AAA. So, off I go back up the hill. At least I know the
hill as I *just* climbed it. I will double-time it as best I can --
no more looking for side trails -- and be back shortly. So back up
the hill I go. I periodically check the phone to see if there is a
signal; 10mins, 15mins no signal yet... 20mins and finally i have
1-bar on the phone. I stop and call AAA and get disconnected/dropped
as soon as the operator connects. Look back down at the phone - it
has switched from digital to analog and then to no-signal. Okay, got
to hike some more. Get to the next switchback, check the phone and
now have 2-bars. Okay, time to try again. Call. Get through... line
is a bit fuzzy but AAA answers.. Yes, Mr. Midnight Boxer let me pull
up your account, your membership looks to be in good standing, your
battery is dead? Okay, lets get someone dispatched to help you out.
Where are you located... click... silence... silence... I look down
at the phone. It is stone cold dead; powered off. I try to power it
back on, nothing. The battery is dead. omg. wtf. I didn't get a
chance to tell them where we are! I am standing on the side of a hill
with a dead cell phone in my hand. My newborn and (up to now, very
loving and tolerant wife) are at the bottom of this hill; and we are
stranded. Now what do I do? Can't do anything but hump back down
this (now f'ing) hill and pray that WhisperFoot brought her cellphone
AND has it charged ((she has a history of not (a) bringing it and not
(b) keeping it charged)).

Slipping and sliding, I get to the bottom of the hill. Muddy; wet;
who really cares at this point. Honey, you REALLY aren't going to
believe this.....please tell me you have your phone. you DO? ....is
it ...charged? great (...too tired at this point to muster any real
enthusiasm, I know that I will have to climb that abominable hill
again). "How much signal do you have?" "None." "Yeah, that is
about right...." and back up I go. This time I am successful in
getting through to AAA and getting a service truck dispatched. Before
hanging-up, the dispatcher tells me the estimated time it will take
the service truck: 2hrs. 2HRS!?, Oh, thats right, it's Thanksgiving
Day and *everyone* is off enjoying the holiday... back down the hill.

To conclude this flashback, the AAA truck arrived 1.5hrs later and
what should have been a 15min drive-by-box became a 4-5hr detour into
a letterboxing-hell and a general Holiday nightmare. We drove
straight home and did make Turkey-Day dinner with the family, but we
were not really in the holiday mood the rest of the night (not
terribly unexpected, right?). As I recollect it, neither of us joined
into the usual internecine-intrafamily political debates (I suppose
the family chalked it up to our being overly-stressed new parents --
they had no idea of the 1st half our our day and the *fun* we had at
the reservoir).

So, getting back to today: we revisited the scene of this nightmare.
Today's outing, like the previous outing was part of a larger plan to
grab other boxes in the area AND was a rainy nasty day (which, of
course, is totally appropriate). While parking and suiting-up, we
were cracking jokes about how much fun it would be to leave the cars
lights on (for the record WhisperFoot did not leave the car lights on
today!). And, as I was intimately (overly) familiar with the clues
and the hill, WhisperFoot took the lead and took us right to the box.
It turns out that the clues were very, very vague and misleading
about the "3/4 up the trail" --- "up the trail" is subjective. The
Planter really meant "3/4 up to the first switchback" to a side trail,
not "3/4 up the trail", of which I never actually found the end of...
the fire road continued on for miles into the wilderness. While
stamping-in, I relished in other boxers notes talking about how vague
the clues were and how they had walked MUCH further up the main hill
than necessary... at least I am not the only one that had trouble with
the clues! :)

Anyway, that is the story. What should have been a 15min drive-by
turned into one of the worst letterboxing days we have ever had which,
after the passage of time, has turned into a funny anecdotal story
that only a fellow letterboxer would truely understand. Looking back
this box was truely one of "Life's Little Adventures"! And, at least
from today's perspective, I am happy to have experienced it. As a
side-note: I am happy to report that today I did *not* burn the
letterbox in a Kingsford pyre, as I threatened after humping up and
down that hill three times in a soaking cold rain...

I am sure that all hikers and 'boxers, no matter how seasoned, have
their own Stories.


:)
Midnight Boxer
