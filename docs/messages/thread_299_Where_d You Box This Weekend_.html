Dangerous creatures

From: Silent Doug | Date: 2004-08-23 15:22:46 UTC
At 09:23 AM 8/23/2004, you wrote:
>Anyway, here is a topic for the week....
>
>What is the most dangerous creature you have encountered while boxing?

On one of my first letterboxing forays, I ran into some hikers coming the
other way down a mountain who told me that they had seen a bear off the
trail not too far ahead of me. One of them seemed to think it was far
enough off the trail and moving away that I wouldn't run into it if I
continued up the trail, a second said that she wouldn't go up that trail,
and a third didn't have an opinion one way or the other. I discussed it
with them some more and decided that the bear hadn't been acting in any
threatening way, so I continued up the trail, making lots of noise to make
my presence know.

I was moving pretty quickly, though, and my ears and eyes were attuned to
every noise in the woods -- and the chipmunks were particularly active (it
seems), so I kept jumping at every rustle in the leaves off the trail.
Finally, I stopped to catch my breath, kneeling on a log by the side of the
trail. That's when I noticed a movement off to my left on the other side of
the trail -- and saw a rattlesnake moving slowly directly down the side of
the path away from me. I froze in place, and it continued on, unfazed by my
presence. After it had moved on, I continued up the mountain, again moving
as quickly as I could to put distance between myself and the bear and now
the rattlesnake, and then came upon a very large black rat snake, right in
the middle of the trail. They're mostly harmless, but it was still big
enough that, if provoked, he would have made sure that I remembered him,
and I was moving so quickly that I really skidded when I put on the brakes
after seeing him.

The damn chipmunks were really getting on my nerves, and then I turned a
corner and startled a flock of wild turkeys, who made tracks into the
woods, but not without giving me another good startle (since my senses were
by now on very high alert). When I finally got near the top of the
mountain, I was surprised again, this time by a white tail buck standing
about 15 feet away from me. He took one look, didn't like what he saw, and
bolted in the opposite direction.

I never saw the bear, but needless to say I wasn't able to avoid feeling
his presence in the woods. And after all that, I wasn't able to find the
letterbox there!

The spiders here in PA can get pretty big, but they're nothing compared to
the big wolf spider I found when I uncovered a letterbox (or geocache) in
Florida. They're supposedly harmless, but I was sure not to get close
enough to find out.

And of course the bee or wasp or yellow jacket that stung me a couple of
weeks ago has to be on my list, since I apparently shrieked "like a girl"
when I was stung....

From: Lightnin Bug | Date: 2004-08-23 15:51:20 UTC
We've been pretty lucky letterboxing, with the scariest thing we
encountered was a couple squirrels.

Now, hiking and MTB'ing is another thing.

I came across a bear cub (which quickly bolted off into the woods)
while hiking the AT coming down from Sunfish Pond in the Jersey side
of the Delaware Water Gap. I was with a bunch of folks whoe thought
that I was kidding.

I nearly ran over a timber rattler once while MTB'ing on the Lehigh
Gorge Trail about 10 miles out from Jim Thorpe. Coiled up, buzzing
and ready to strike! That *WAS* unsettling, so I can imagine how you
felt first getting hyper aware of chipmunks and then running into
your rattler.

Moral is, lets be careful out there, especially when getting into
nooks and crannies.

Lightnin' Bug

A few weeks ago I had a nasty run in with some yellow jackets.  I was at Ringing Rocks, making my way across the field, pounding on every rock I came across, when while standing on one rock I heard a buzzing noise.  I looked up thinking it was an airplane flying over, but saw none.  I then looked down and saw about four or five yellow jackets.  After a quick explicative, I shifted my weight to jump away, but ended up shifting the whole rock.  Now, instead of four or five bees, there were forty of fifty bees.  Swiftly I leapt from rock to rock until I reached the end of the field.  I did not know I could move that fast anymore.  I managed to get away with only three of four stings, most of the bees went for my legs which were stress from the leaping and resisted them.
