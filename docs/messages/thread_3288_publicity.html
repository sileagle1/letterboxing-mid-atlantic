This is the thing I don't get in the whole discussion of AQ and
publicity and what not. When folks first start letterboxing, where do
they go first? What site comes up first on every search engine? Some
folks don't find out about AQ until long after they have started boxing,
they use letterboxing.org. While there is much on AQ that has nothing to
do with letterboxing, and much that I would be happy to see just thrown
in the trash heap, and many members there that are a bunch of idiots, I
am not ready to throw the site under the bus.

What I do like about the site is the search vehicle that is used, it is
much easier to find boxes, to narrow searches, ect then on
letterboxing.org. If I had just that ability I would be happy as a clam.
Also, Ryan does seem to at least respond to user concerns, which is much
more then I can say Chio and company over at letterboxing.org. That is
why I use AQ and not letterboxing.org.

And, because of that, that is why my boxes tend to get fewer finders
then many other area boxes. Not because they are harder, some are very
straight forward and meant for the new boxer, but because the clues are
not listed on letterboxing.org. If you want to keep new boxers from
finding your boxes, in my experience, listing on just AQ will do just that.

As for the publicity aspect, those that say it will be the ruin of
letterboxing are full of it (sorry June, not pointing fingers, just
saying). The hobby got started in the US because of a magazine article,
and now bears little resemblance to its British cousin. There are a load
of boxers that found out about it by reading of it in places like Family
Fun and they are great people, and plant great boxes. I have never found
any correlation between news articles and the destruction of boxes.
People just don't do it. Sure, you get folks that go out there, think it
is great fun, and don't rehide well, ect, ect, but that is going to
happen no matter what, and has happened since the beginning.

That's my $2 worth.

As arguably the most obnoxiously vocal advocate of avoiding publicity for the game, I will
once again stand for the pasting.

My position against publicity is to urge letterboxers to show restraint when dealing with
the press. I am not arguing for silence, just thoughtful restraint.

My biggest concern is not that we will get a flood of 'noxers trashing the boxes. My
concern is that we currently operate under the radar of the managers of most parks and
recreation areas where we "play." We benefit from a benign neglect because we - in
general - have been respectful in our operation.

It is my belief however, that if the profile of letterboxing ever reaches a national or
regional consciousness level (what people in publicity call a tipping point) park managers
will be forced to decide to either permit or ban letterboxing.

What do you think most will decide? What was decided by the National Parks? Do you
think most responsible land managers will opt for allowing unrestricted dumping of
unsupervised plasticware in their park?

So I advocate restraint. Go ahead, talk to your local Boy Scout troop or hold a seminar at
the local crafts club. Tell your friends. A camping magazine calls? Fine - talk to them.
The local shopper? You bet.

But if the Philadelphia Inquirer or Newsweek calls, I would respectfully suggest you think
twice. The story you tell them might not be the story they print.

As someone who has worked in the media for over 20 years I can tell you I have seen a
number of "happy puff pieces" become "threat or menace" pieces on a slow news day.

I have respectfully requested that digphilly.com remove my copyrighted material from
their web site. Not because I am afraid of a horde of proto-boxers, but because it is mine
and if I don't want it on AQ, I certainly don't want it on diggphilly.com

My 2

With all due respect, that is the line they teach reporters in journalism school to use to get
people to cooperate. "You want people to hear your side of the story, right? You killed all
those people for a reason!" It's a puff piece. If enough people say "no thank you" they will
move on to easier pickings in the world of outdoor recreation. It's not the Lindbergh baby
- oh, I'm showing my age - It's not Lindsay Lohan. They won't pursue it like hounds.

I am reminded of Scarab's story of working with the Time magazine reporter on their
letterboxing piece. He was also lucky in that the reporter let him review the piece before
printing. One of his boxes was described as being near the entrance to the U.S. State
Department. I don't know if you all remember what the climate was like in America right
after 9-11, but a hidden box outside the U.S. State department would certainly have
raised some eyebrows. Scarab successfully convinced the reporter to change the
description of the location. What if he hadn't? Not the end, I'm sure, but another 2 inches
closer to the radar, perhaps.

When I was a kid they invented this cool game called Dungeons and Dragons. Yes. I
admit it. I was a D&D geek. But goldarnit I was a pioneering D&D geek! A few years after

playing D&D. Then Pat Robertson piled on claiming it was a pathway to Satanism so he
could hit up his flock for more cash. Next thing you know, graph paper and multi-sided
dice were banned at my high school and many others across the country. No D&D in
school.

It happens that fast. It doesn't take a very creative mind to see how our little thing could
be perceived as dangerous to kids or dangerous to the environment or dangerous to
public safety by those who should have better things to do.

Perhaps I should ask what we gain by publicity. Do we need to be preaching this gospel?
Sure I want more boxes. We all do.

But what is wrong with steady controlled growth? It's good for forests, why not
letterboxing? Word-of-mouth, person-to-person or person to small groups can help
improve the quality of letterboxing for everyone. You get people who are involved in
perpetuating the traditions and mores of the game - People like you and me and most of
the fine folks here on LBMA.

This is why I urge restraint.

Without offering an opinion one way or another on this
question, I've always liked this article, simply as a
way of thinking about the question --

http://www.templetons.com/brad/linkright.html

It is a very old article, and no doubt more modern case
law and articles have been written since, but there it is,
FWIW.

My personal opinion is that the owners of web content
should have some rights to say how it is used, that
is -- just because this medium happens to allow an
intellectual property free-for-all, doesn't mean it
should. I think that is an artefact of the technology,
not the intent.

It would be nice if people were simply courteous on these
matters and respected the IP owners' wishes, regardless of
whether it is legal or not. One thing that seems clear
about the article I link to above -- the IP owner must make
it clear that they do not wish to be linked to in the
first place.

I'm awake, my friend.

In my case, it was not a link. They copied my clue and published to the web on their site.
Their site sells advertising and is therefore a commercial site using my intellectual
property for profit without permission. That is not covered by "Fair Use" protection of the
Copyright Act. The clue is not used in a news story as defined by the law as relevent to
current events, nor is it used in a review. Those exceptions are made in copyright law.
That is not the case here.

My copyright exists as soon as I publish whether I put a little c. next to it or not. I do have
a copyright page on my site explaining my exclusive rights to the material contained on
the site.

Materials used by the general public on Facebook and MySpace are for non-commmercial
purposes. In the exceptions where the material is used for commercial promotional
purposes, the materials are copyrighted by the owner or given clearance before
publication. MySpace and FaceBook are also involved in ongoing copyright litigation over
these matters.

Most of the law regarding intellectual property has yet to be written. The technology - as
usual - has outpaced the law. What the law will be in the future will be decided by what
we decide is acceptable today. If we take an "everybody's doing it" attitude there will be no
copyright protection for anyone - except those with pockets deep enough to drag you into
court.

I am not suing diggphilly.com. I am requesting they remove my material from their site.
If they refuse, I will have a discussion with them. If they still refuse I will have a
discussion with their advertisers. I find that is most effective - much more so for a small
outfit like diggphilly who won't like their advertisers bothered. I might also buttonhole the
owner at the next Philly Ad Club meeting and explain in front of his peers that he is using
copyrighted material illegally. That should go over pretty big.

I may even recruit some fellow letterboxers who agree with me to call and express their
distress at these matters to diggphilly or their advertisers. Any volunteers?

I can't control what happens to the plastic litter I leave in the woods once I leave it there.
In the grand tradition of the ridiculous pollution credits developed by industry, I clean up
more trash from the environment than I leave.

And yes, I often violate property owners rights by hiding letterboxes on private property.

Ok - I am waay late to the party here. I have not posted in weeks.
Work has been hell and thankfully it was broken up by a nice
Carribean cruise were the family and I planted 3 boxes (I couldn't
resist) but now it was back to work again. As far as the controversy
here - or seeming controversy...I'd have to say that I agree with a
lot of the comments here. The trouble is that I agree with opposing
ones... ;) I didn't really like the Youtube video - but I myself
learned of letterboxing from a Family Fun article about 3 years ago -
so who am I to cast stones... But, the difference to me is that I
took to LB'ing like an old soul as soon as I got my feet wet. I
respected the history (and researched it extensively) and then I
practiced good LB'ing habits -lest I not be able to preach them to
others ;) I often find that I agree with Brian's viewpoint (TGD) but
then also with Nathan's. Since I have met you both I can see that
you practice what you preach as well. While there are some parts of
AQ that I don't use, I like other parts and when I met Ryan at
Nathan's gather - I could see that he was also a 'good guy' as well
as everyone else I have met through LB'ing. He does not seem like
the evil twin of LB'ing who only wants your $$ as some people often
say he is. I think he does a good job in general - so a few months
back - he got more of my $. :-) I have yet to meet Randy but that
will probably happen eventually and I'm sure I'll see that there are
reasons why I agree with his viewpoints also (and spend so many
friggin hours looking for his boxes!). And that's just the thing,
it's not that hard to find parts of everyone's viewpoint here that
can be agreed with. If we weren't all somewhat like minded in
general - when it comes to letterboxing, this discussion would
probably not be going on. There is a certain amount of 'caring' that
the people have here towards this hobby (obsession) of ours. It is
those 'other' people who trample all over the hobby, or mangle boxes
assuming they are geo-junk, or in general just don't take the time to
bother to see what LB'ing is all about - those are the people (I
think of them as LB frauds) that usually cause the problems that we
rant about. Now, although there may be some disagreements here - I
don't believe we have any Letterbox-frauds in this venue.
Although...I could be mistaken... ;) On another note, we found
Otis' Friends "Fearsome Fish" today. After a marathon day of family
adventure and driving all over New Jersey, we finally wound up
snagging that recently planted little gem. Thanks - John
