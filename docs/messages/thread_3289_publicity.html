I have to chime in on this one!
 
I agree with Miss Moon 100%. (now that's something I never thought I'd say!)  :-)
Many of us have had the opportunity to "sing the praises" of our favorite hobby.
But why? Publicity about letterboxing was equated to the following from an enigma from the north:
 
"It's like having your own private fishing hole - teaming with fat, plump trout in an idyllic setting. Then you tell your friends about it; who tell other friends, etc. By the next time you return, the fish are gone, the wild flowers are trampled and there is litter all over."
 
I see this as almost a generational thing (although age doesn't really enter into this - it's more like the years you've been letterboxing.) Those of us who have been boxing for 3+ years feel differently about bells & whistles and publicity. Those new to this hobby know nothing different than all that Atlas Quest has to offer.
 
I was on Atlas Quest several months ago and happened to read a post where Erik Davis was mentioned. "Who is Erik Davis" several of the newbies mentioned? That really shocked me.
 
Like Miss Moon (I've said it again!) I do enjoy some of the bells on AQ - the map that shows where some of the boxes are located within a town. Makes for effiecient planning when visiting new locales but with well over 2000 finds, Sue & I have done fine without it though all those years prior to Ryan develoing this. But it is kinda cool.
 
Maybe we (the vets) need to do a better job with educating those new to the hobby. It's something we've tried to do on the Newboxers list. But - newbies need to have the desire to learn. Therein may lie the rub.
 
Thanks for reading my post. I'm enjoying this thread.

From: Randy Hall | Date: 2008-07-28 13:50:43 UTC
> Maybe we (the vets) need to do a better job with educating those new to the hobby.

I disagree (since no one educated me and I made up my own rules, I
guess I'm biased). Newbies (and anyone else), are free to enjoy
the hobby as they please, within the bounds of certain ethics such
as LNT, courtesy, etc., which are a different scope than this
conservation.

I even think Ryan (or anyone else) is entitled to make a buck, if such
a market exists. I'm not aware of too many people who don't sell some
sort of good or service (and those who don't, IMHO, have little right
to judge those who must). Everyone, be they buyer or purveyor of
schlock, has their own gods to answer to, not me.

So, I don't really agree that some sort of veteran elite should attempt
to keep the hobby in some mold that it was when they joined (not that
I'm necessarily suggesting that that is happening, but I come across
that way sometimes, I suppose). They, however, may want to stay in
that mold, and see how the market of ideas treats them, and make sure
they are comfortable answering to their own gods.

I do agree with one thing however, from my oft-quoted Don Henley song --

"They call it paradise, I don't know why"
"You call someplace paradise, kiss it goodbye".

Remember, tho, in that song, the ultimate condemnation is on the buyers,
not the purveyors.

Everyone whines about Wal*Mart (at least they did when they came to
Kennett Square), but at the of the day, people vote with their feet
and wallets, don't they? I don't shop at Wal*Mart, but who am I to
judge? Is AQ the Wal*Mart of letterboxing? What would that make
LbNA?

Yes, I to am enjoying this thread. All in good fun of course :)

Cheers
Randy

WoL scared me--I didn't like the site, but the thing that scared me

writing on the wall. Things are going to change whether you like it or
not. At least this way, I was able to help direct some of the changes
into things that made more sense.

Such as not counting postals, virtuals, and other non-letterboxes as
part of the PFX count. Such as setting up a rating system that doesn't
point out the *worst* rated boxes out there. Such as simple icons
rather than a complex encoding system that needed to decoder to
understand.

It was a terrible website, and I hated the idea of it getting any
traction, so yes, I was very much against it, and I don't miss it. But
it should have been a wake-up call for everyone on LbNA, and I'm
rather disappointed to see that didn't happen. The folks at
geocaching.com bought the domain at letterboxer.com--did you know
that? Try typing letterboxer.com into your browser. I'd have been
appalled if they developed a "letterboxing" website, and I like to
think that perhaps, maybe, just because Atlas Quest exists with all
those bells and whistles a lot of people like, maybe that's the reason
they never did anything with the domain--something that could have
been far worse than you'll find on AQ.

And, heck, I make up a lot of stuff along the way. Originally, I just
wanted to practice my programming skills and create a site so people
could log all their finds online. A lot of people were doing it anyhow
on their personal websites, and it seemed like it would be nice if
they were all in one place to make it easier to find. I would have
never guessed it would grow into what it has today nor that it would
ever make enough money to pay my bills.

Rather a nice surprise for me, as it turned out--the best job I've
ever had. =) Anyone who thinks I do it just for the money is sadly
mistaken, though. Last year, I didn't even make half of what I did
working at Intel in 2001. The pay sucks.

say about me that I don't feel are accurate such as being a hypocrite

Back in the old days, when someone complained on the talk list about

some other hobby." I never liked to challenge such statements because
invariably it was always said by people I considered friends, but it
bothered me. To me, letterboxing should be open to anyone and everyone
who wants to participate. Regardless of age, gender, race, religion,
physical fitness--it doesn't matter. I feel it should be open to
everyone, and kicking someone out just because they think the hobby
can be improved is a bad precedent.

I'm rather happy to see that such statements rarely happen anymore. =)
LbNA is not for everyone--it's not for people who embrace changes or
growth. Before those people had no where else to go, but now they do.

are listed on letterboxing.org allowing you to enjoy their work as well.

I also feel there's still room for several niche letterboxing
websites. Atlas Quest I try to design to accommodate as wide variety
of people as I can, but not everyone enjoys a website that tries to be
everything to everyone, and there's still room for niche websites that
fill those needs for people who'd like to letterbox but find that
neither Atlas Quest nor LbNA suit their needs.
