Tragic Trail Tale

From: Hikers & Hounds | Date: 2008-08-03 22:41:40 UTC
Did you folks read about the hiker in Washington State who was shot and killed by a hunter this morning? She was hiking with a friend and stooped to put somethig in her backpack and a teenage hunter, who was with an adult, mistook her for a bear and shot and killed her. I am really confused, not being a hunter myself and knowing nothing about guns. But wouldn't he have had to line up his shot and if so how could he not tell it was a human and not an animal? What sort of clothing might she have been wearing that he mistook for bear fur? It just seems so bizarre. Plus I wonder when Bear season is in Wahsington State. And I wonder if I should wear orange blaze year round in case there are some knuckleheads who don't obey the season rules.
 
this year I've been so busy at work I can['t remember when I've been on trail last, probably in Bermuda. But I hope to get active again next year and stories like this make me re-think the whole blaze orange thing. I don't care if people think I'm an idiot if I wear it in August, at least I'll be a live idiot.
 
Anywa, pretty sad story and something to think about. Unless everyone wants to return to the lfaming about publicity. Oh, I can't believe I did that!

From: Katrina Lustofin | Date: 2008-08-04 12:11:28 UTC
Sometimes hunters don't really look before shooting.  My ex-boyfriend got shot while out hunting with his uncle - by his uncle.  His uncle saw movement, assumed it was a deer, and fired.  Odd thing is, he and all his cousins still hunt with this uncle.

Walmart here has orange safety vests for dogs marketed by the ASPCA and on sale for a dollar each.  I picked up two this weekend in preparation for hunting season, because I'm always worried that the dogs will be mistaken for deer.  I guess I should pick up a vest for me, too.  And I'll continue to try to box and hike in areas that don't allow hunting or on days when hunting isn't allowed.

Katy

Of course.  Still, that's cold comfort to the victims of the bad apples - again, a good reason for precautionary measures.  Almost by definition, we're not dealing with the sharpest tools in the shed -- or the blind.  If you really want to freak yourself out, consider the case of Higgins v. Walls, involving a motorist in Delaware shot by a trespassing hunter (and convicted felon) while driving down Route 113.  There's no accounting for stupid.
 
There are some who say that people tend to fear unlikely events more than ordinary ones: 
 
http://psychologytoday.com/articles/index.php?term=20071228-000005&page=1
 
Interestingly, many who might take extra precautions to avoid a hunting accident would probably underestimate the risk of tomatoing out:
 
http://psychologytoday.com/articles/pto-19941101-000027.html
 
I still think it's a good idea to wear brightly colored clothing anywhere a really stupid person might mistake you with game.
