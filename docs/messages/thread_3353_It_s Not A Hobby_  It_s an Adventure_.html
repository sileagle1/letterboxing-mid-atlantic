From Sixinthesticks:

-I posted this story on a yahoo letterboxing group. I thought you might like to read how

OK, I thought you might enjoy a newbie story. Laugh away-

We six thought it would be fun to show some neighbors how to
letterbox. My oldest 2 planned the boxes and clues and went out and
hid them in our neighborhood. We invited 4 kids who were 9, 8, 6,
and 3 years old. (I figured it would give their mom a peaceful
afternoon and I'm used to some chaos.) After we had lunch and found
our boxes, the boys decided we should find a "real" letterbox. There
are a couple easy ones near our house that we had already found but I
was afraid my 10 year old would just walk right to the box and not
let them have the fun of finding it. I grabbed a clue that I
remembered as sounding child friendly and we headed out the door- me
and six kiddos- ages 10, 9, 8, 6, 3 and 2, all boys except the 3
y/o. Read that again, one "responsible adult" and 6 kids under that
age of 11. We found the right starting place and parked the van. The
brothers assured me that they had gone up this path before with their
parents and sister so I figured we would be fine.

After an hour of hiking/carrying the 2 or 3 year old, up and down
ridges and gulleys (was that a "most difficult" hiking trail sign we
passed?) I told them we should turn around. (I'm not only slow, but
very bright) All I heard was, "We can't go back! We didn't find
it!" Another hour (yes, HOUR) brought us to where we thought the box
should be.
It was no where to be found. I wrestled the clue back from one of
the boys and realized we hadn't gone FAR enough. After another 20
minutes of alternately carrying the little ones, the boys found the
box. The little ones sat down, totally spent and not willing to take
another step. The boys scouted ahead and said that it looked like
the trail would head down to the road, instead of hiking all the way
back. As I stamped in, the sky grew completely black. Oh, no. I
quickly finished and made sure the box was back when the lightning
and thunder started. I have never heard a little girl shriek like
that in my life. "I WANT MY MOMMY!!!!!!! " "AAAAAHHHHHH! !!" I want
your mommy too, Sweetie, or mine! The shrieking continued and down
the hill we went.

Suddenly the pack of boys in front of me stopped. "What's the
matter?" "We heard a crash- right over there- like something moving."

Above the shrieking I yelled, "If you think that is a bear or a deer
trying to get us, you're crazy. They are running for their dear
lives because they are scared to death! KEEP HIKING!" We reached
the bottom of the hill and could see the road a scant 30 feet away.
Wonderful, right? Nope, swamp. Couldn't get around it with that
crew. Back on the trail, cross the bridge and I think I can see a
path to the road. I hike the 2 yo old up on to my shoulders and
bushwack through berry bryers up to my neck. The road! I went back
and carried each one over the bryers and we were on flat solid
ground. In the pouring rain. With lightning and thunder. And 2
toddlers in my arms.

We walked about half a mile back before a couple of cars pulled
over. The first one I walked up to had a white haired lady with an
immaculately clean car. We looked like we had rolled down the hill
but this looked like a safe bet for hitchhiking to me. She didn't
mind and I piled the kids on top of each other in the back seat.

-Yes, their mother is still talking to me.
-No, I don't recommend this to be done by non-professionals.
-Please make sure to double check the clue and take the RIGHT one.
-Check the weather before leaving.
-The doctor will give you a nice shot in the arm if your eye swells
shut from poison ivy.
-Six year olds always touch the poison ivy, and your neck, and your
face.
