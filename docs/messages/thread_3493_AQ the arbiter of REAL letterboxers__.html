From: Randy Hall | Date: 2009-02-20 13:41:22 UTC
> Miz Scarlet is right. There are no rules, just get out there and box.
> Make em' hard, make em' easy, make them unaccessible, make them for
> everyone,

Ok, I'll play devil's advocate then, without actually looking at the
clue in question or being familiar with the individual. These
comments are intended to be abstract.

How would one feel of a box with a restriction that only those of
San descent [choose your ethnicity here] were permitted or not
permitted to find it? Suppose some web site were programmed to
enforce these ethnic restrictions?

Of course the creator can do what they want, and put such a restriction
on it. Of course the creator can use a web site that enforces such
restrictions. There are no rules. But, would such be offensive? (note
there is a difference between "rules" and "social etiquette").

I think it would be offensive to some people. Would it be offensive
to a preponderance of the letterboxing population? Would it be
"crossing a line"? Who knows? This is only a thought experiment.

My opinion is that you "cross the line" when you place restrictions
outside the problem domain of the box or clue. Requiring someone to
find 10 unrelated boxes, or be a member of some group or web site
is outside the problem domain of most boxes, and thus I find it
offensive. Requiring people to find _that_ clue, or solve rock
trivia questions related to a particular clue, however, are inside
the problem domain of that and related boxes, and thus are not
offensive. Solving the mystery of a mystery box is fine, being
San or joining some web site is not.

It is all about line drawing, and perhaps some (perhaps a very tiny
minority), find that this sort of thing crosses it.

JMHO, of course
Randy
not a "real" letterboxer, but never short on pretentious answers

While I have sympathy for the devil's viewpoint and am closer to it than not, allow me to retort.
 
Comparisons to discrete and insular minorities of the sort described in footnote 4 of Carolene Products, or racial and ethnic distinctions of any kind, seem inapt here.  The filter at issue is based not on innate characteristics such as race, gender, age, etc., but on conduct on a web page open to those of any race, religion, or creed.  That filter is at least as porous as one in which, say, clues are posted in recipes on a cooking site that requires registration.  And, while, for example, most people would be offended by a rule that only San could practice medicine, few would object to education and licensing requirements.  One might object to certain licensing requirements as pointless, ineffective, or otherwise misguided, but that's another issue.
 
There is a tightrope balance here between equality of access to clues and opportunity to solve them (and I agree that a level playing field is something to strive for as a placer), and secrecy.  It's not just a suspicion of The Man.  Secrets are fun. 
 
Telling people that you have a secret that you do not plan to share with them, though, is not only obnoxious, but counterproductive.  That's where our friend from the Sunshine State went off the rails.

From: EllBee | Date: 2009-02-21 06:06:48 UTC
I use AQ restrictions to prevent clues being cached by search engines,
not to prevent folks who want the clues from getting to them. When I
started boxing I listed my boxes on LbNA. Then I listed them on LbNA and
AQ. Then AQ implemented find restrictions, and I used them, but still
listed my boxes on LbNA. Over time, it became clear that there was a
dissonance in listing boxes on LbNA that couldn't be accessed by
everyone because of AQ-based restrictions, so I pulled my box listings
from LbNA. It didn't hurt that it got to be too much work to maintain
clues on both sites. ;->

I was never trying to prevent any boxers from accessing my clues. If I
was contacted for clues by someone who was stymied by the AQ
restrictions, I freely offered the clues. If anyone decides not to
bother to ask because of how I choose to list my boxes, well, fine. Your
choice.

I'd like to make it clear that Ryan has been very clear from day one
that he didn't appreciate boxes being listed on LbNA that had AQ
restrictions. And for those who are unclear on this point, you do not
need to register at AQ in order to access clues. Anyone who visits the
website can get clues - you don't have to join - what registration
allows you to do is log finds and plants. By contrast, last time I
checked, you have to join LbMA in order to have access to the clues that
appear in the posts and files section.

From: wilmcoe@yahoo.com | Date: 2009-02-21 08:30:08 UTC
You don't need to be registered at AQ to access MOST of the clues.  But the clues in question have a find restriction of 1.  I cannot access those clues if I am not logged in under my AQ identity.  My alter ego cannot access those clues when logged in under "her" AQ identity UNTIL that identity logs first logs a find on another box.  "Anyone who visits the website" does not have access to THOSE clues unless they register, log at least one find and are logged in to the website.
 
By contrast, you do not need to be registered at letterboxing.org to access ANY of the clues.  If you can't access something at letterboxing.org, it's because the planter has linked to another website that is restricting you.
 
The restrictions work on AQ because unless you already meet the criteria, you are not aware that the box even exists.  It's not being flaunted in your face, "here's a box but you can't have the clues."  Pretty much the same with the LBMA boxes, if you are a member you have full access, if not you don't even know they are there.  The problems with the listings under discussion are a) they say "here's a clue but you can't get it if you don't meet the restrictions on this other website" and b) they imply that if you don't meet those restrictions, you're not a "real letterboxer." 
 
BTW there are other options to hide boxes from search engines, besides the plant & find restrictions....

legitimate boxers, and is a bad economic bet. If you have 100
boxers looking for the clue, and each must incur this increased
cost of registering on AQ and writing a script to jack their F count,

the game by large web sites, and the commercial mechanisms on
(at least some) of those sites. Both of these are personal
