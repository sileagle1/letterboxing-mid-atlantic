From: nattybumppolbna | Date: 2009-03-12 18:00:25 UTC
If you are going to ask permission - which I very rarely do and advise against - I would go to the meeting prepared.

Have your 10 second description of letterboxing down pat. Your tongue can get tangled when you try to ad lib a description of what we do. It's good to include the concepts of "educational" "family-friendly" and "ecologically conscious."

I like visual aids. I take along copies of the Smithsonian and Time magazine articles to show it is a more substantial activity than just me and a couple of buddies playing hide -n- seek.
I also like to take along examples of boxes and clues. Small and eco-friendly as you can make them.
Also if you have an idea of the box/clue you will be placing, discuss it with them. If you can get them making suggestions, you are taking a big step toward buy-in.

I have a 50% success rate with permissions from libraries and local (county/town) parks and 0% success with federal & State parks. 75%+ in bars.

Natt-

I disagree........no surprise here huh?.....with getting permission. And if you know how to hide it, there is no reason why anyone ever has to know it's there. I have become a fan (though I've only done one so far) of the magnetic, no logbook letterbox which consists of a stamp with a backing glued on (unless you don't do backings--in which case skip that) and the magnet glued to that. Nice and flat and unobtrusive. Most shelves in libraries are metal and if not, use sticky backed velcro. I've had one outside in a location where I could never put a logbook and it's working great.
 
I put in the clues that there is no logbook so anyone who doesn't want to do it can skip it. It's been found quite a few times and no complaints yet. If boxes in series can not have logbooks in all of them I feel I can not have a logbook if it helps to keep it hidden. If anyone at the library finds it for some reason it can just remain a mystery as to what it is. Unless they just happen to know about boxing. I live in New York City and my small neighborhood library no cameras. I think lots of libraries don't.
