From: Lightnin Bug | Date: 2009-07-14 22:30:14 UTC
I made the mistake of walking through a grassy field at Peace (Tick) Valley park back in mid may - Pulled 9 ticks off of me at various times that day and a deer tick that had latched on to a love handle. It was mostly dead having been crushed by my jeans an belt that I had put on after showering. Watched the area for bulls-eye, but the little stinker was only on me for a few hours and thankfully nothing occurred. They need 24-36 hours to transmit Lyme (if they are infected).

Always do a tick check with a loved one.

DEET (N,N-Diethyl-meta-toluamide) is commonly known as the king of mosquito repellents, though not everyone is keen to slather it on their skin. A study conducted in the late 1980s on Everglades National Park employees to determine the effects of DEET found that a full one-quarter of the subjects studied experienced negative health effects that they blamed on exposure to the chemical. Effects included rashes, skin irritation, numb or burning lips, nausea, headaches, dizziness and difficulty concentrating.

Duke University pharmacologist Mohamed Abou-Donia, in studies on rats, found that frequent and prolonged DEET exposure led to diffuse brain cell death and behavioral changes, and concluded that humans should stay away from products containing it. But other studies have shown that while a few people have sensitivity to DEET applications, most are unaffected when they use DEET products on a sporadic basis according to the instructions on the label.

Re: [lbma] Re: ticks (Was "ticks". . . Now... "Anti-Tick Body Armor!" )

From: Katrina Lustofin | Date: 2009-07-16 04:04:49 UTC
While I will not argue that DEET is 100% safe (heck, even water can be toxic, given the correct circumstances), there is NO scientific evidence that DEET causes cancer or any of the effects the employees of the Everglades attribute to DEET.  Notice that article doesn't actually say that DEET causes those effects, just that the employes claim *they* thought the symptoms were from DEET exposure.  If you want to know what the toxicology studies and over 45 years of use have found out about DEET, read what the EPA or ATSDR (Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry) have to say.  These are organizations that are in place to protect us and the environment.   DEET was re-registered in 1998 and underwent rigorous toxicological testing for this.  An interesting aside - aspirin would not be licensed for use today if it were not grandfathered in because it would not be able to pass the current testing procedures.

As for the seizures, if you look at the data 1) there is no *causitive* link between the seizure and DEET use and 2) the rate of seizure following DEET use is actually lower than the rate of afebrile seizures in children. 

If you are totally set against using DEET, the CDC and EPA both have recommendations for several different insect repellents, including newer and botanical repellents.  I have not read or heard of any seizures antecdotes about these products, but keep in mind that these products have only been used for the past 5-10 years, compared with DEET's 45+ year history of use.  And any of these products will also help to product you against not only Lyme disease, but also other tick and mosquito-borne diseases.

Katy
