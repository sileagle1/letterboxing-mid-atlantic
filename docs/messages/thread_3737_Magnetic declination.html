I rarely pull out my compass when letterboxing. I have a good sense of direction and the planter usual indicates a clear landmark. ie 90 degrees to a "V" tree. I look eastish for a "V".

Re: [lbma] Magnetic declination

From: von der Insel | Date: 2010-03-27 22:53:15 UTC
I've only had to do declination conversion going from map azimuth to compass azimuth, and then only in the Army.  LB "rules" - if there truly are any - wants the clue writers to post the magnetic azimuths, as that's what folks will be needing on the trail.  I'd only need to convert if I'm using my USGS maps to find a quick-and-easy way to a certain spot.  But I never do that because
1) I might miss something neat that the planter has me walk by
2) it's cheating (okay, Special Forces say that "if you're not cheating, you're not trying" - I know)
3) I'm not in such a daggone hurry when letterboxing.  It's a hobby!

From: Ron | Date: 2010-03-28 02:24:50 UTC
Same here, only in the Army. I would also assume a GPS user would not consider that a compass user might not have done a conversion not thinking that a boxer probably isn't using a topo map with the deviation printed on it. if how I put that makes any sense <grin>.

What I heard at For Benning from the Rangers was "Son, if you ain't cheating, you ain't trying, and if you ain't trying,  you just ain't !@#$ going to make it." So I proceeded to put on my snivel gear (a wool sweater) to try and avoid a second case of pneumonia that year. And no, I wasn't caught, avoided the pneumonia, managed to pass land navigation (FT Benning, GA is unique in that the deviation is zero), and graduate. Some still couldn't get it right and didn't. "Almost, almost didn't grad. Didn't grad 'cause he can' nav." ~ Officer Candidate jingle from 1982.

The real important technique is close to following clues: If you can read the topo map, the compass is secondary once you understand where you are supposed to be going and can match the map to the ground. The terrain is better than any compass.

IMHO
 
Ron
