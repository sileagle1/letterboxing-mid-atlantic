Just wanted to toot the horn of the Elba Onions...as usual, we're in
awe!

If you have yet to visit the Western NY region, here's a reason!
Interstate 90, the New York Thruway, has 8 of the best boxes we've
ever SEEN between Buffalo and Syracuse! We were so impressed with the
two we found, we must have giggled like school kids for 10 minutes in
the car at each one. We can't wait to take a trip further east to get
the rest.

The Elba Onions are an ingenious group that's responsible for a good
portion of the boxes in the Western New York area, including a some
great historic cemetary series', so it's no wonder they're at it
again giving us a great search while teaching us local history.

Anyway, it's another reason to visit...come on out for pizza, wings
and a little letterboxing!

Just a note -- you cross-posted the same message to at least 5 or 6
different letterboxing discussion lists. Most people consider cross-posting
a no-no, except in very particular circumstances, and the practice of
cross-posting can quite possibly irk some recipients, particularly if the
topic of the message isn't of specific interest to that group. Northern NY
State is only marginally part of the Mid-Atlantic region, so this message
probably would have been better off not being posted to our Mid-Atlantic
group, IMHO. I just don't want you to start getting heat from some of the
hot-blooded letterboxers out there for cross-posting, which has been known
to happen. Repeated cross-posting has also gotten people banned from some
message boards.

Having said that, I was the first finder of three of those boxes over the
weekend, since I happened to be on a trip to Buffalo from Philly. I found 6
of the 8 altogether; 1 of the others had an unclear clue and the other 1
may be missing already. They were interesting from the perspective of how
to retrieve a letterbox in a highly-trafficked area in broad daylight
without looking suspicious, so I had to variously walk around, talk on my
cell phone, tie my shoelaces (oops, I was wearing loafers, so I adjusted my
socks or pretended like I had rocks in one of my shoes) as I knelt by the
location, wait for people to finish their ice cream or hamburgers and move
out of the area, etc., all while trying to get a visual bead on the
letterbox and then find the precise moment to casually reach down or up and
grab it. The trick was to not look like I was doing anything suspicious or
that I was looking for something, but as I watched what other people were
doing at a service plaza on the thruway, it became clear that lots of
people were stretching their legs, pacing around, hanging out by their
cars, waiting for friends or family to use the restrooms inside, so nothing
that I was doing was any different -- or at least appeared that way to
anybody who happened to be watching. (I also had to cope with the way that
a couple of women were watching me, which led me to believe that I could
have also gotten a couple of phone numbers for my little black letterboxing
log if I'd been so inclined, but fortunately that's not something that most
letterboxers have to deal with).
