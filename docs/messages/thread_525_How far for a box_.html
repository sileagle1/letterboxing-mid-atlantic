How far for a box?

From: gerania93 | Date: 2004-10-27 00:26:15 UTC
I'm thinking of planting a box (probably series) along a dirt road.
Not a bad drive in nice weather. It's a 5 mile loop. However, the
road is a bit of a mess after it rains and may be completely
impassable in the winter and early Spring.

I suppose that some people would schedule a box series like this for
the summer so that they could drive. Others might consider it a nice
little hike.

How many of you would consider walking 5 miles for a box series? How
far have you walked/hiked for a box? As an aside, how long have you
spent looking for a box that you knew 'was around there somewhere'?

For me it was 3 hours. I walked up and down different roads and
trails and toward the end, near the box, discovered that I didn't
have my compass for the directions and paces. I'd been walking for
well over 2 hours and refused to leave till I had the goods. I found
the box. I also lost my cell phone in the woods that day. But I got
the box!

Gillian

From: Myke Scholtes | Date: 2004-10-27 00:45:40 UTC

A 5 mile hike...personally, I wouldn't mind it.  If the clues are to be believed, I just did one yesterday with the South Mountain Series!
 
If I had to choose the manner of my 5-mile hike, though, there's a few things I'd request:
 
1)  Make it a series.  It's a lot more fun to hike a mile...BOX...another mile...BOX...another mile, etc., instead of mile...mile...mile...mile...mile...BOX...pass out.
 
2)  Include lots of landmarks in the clues.  I start second-guessing myself ALL the time on the trail if I haven't seen a "checkpoint" in a while.  While searching for "Stamp Collection," I got really, really frustrated thinking, "I must have missed that first left turn about an hour ago."  (Of course, I hadn't...that's just a really long trail...and NO landmarks in the clues!)
 
3)  Make it something of a loop, so once you find the last box, you don't have to backtrack the whole thing.
 
Those are just my requests, if you want to make it extra pleasant for me.  And you might not want to make it pleasant for me at all, after you see the clues for the next box I'm thinking of planting...  >:)
 
Peace,
Myke

From: Jan | Date: 2004-10-27 01:41:29 UTC
Hi Gillian,
My very first letterbox was on a four and a half mile hike - a little
over two miles to get there and then the same two plus miles back.
There was only one box. It was along a path that I walk all the time
but prior to that I hadn't known about letterboxing. Anyway, it
takes me about one hour and 10 minutes to walk over four miles.

Then a few weeks ago I was searching for the second and third box in
the Nolde Forest series and I walked for over three hours before
finding the third box. I had to come back another day to find the
second one.

I think the point I am trying to make is that I will walk whatever it
takes to find my little treasures. A five mile walk for a series of
boxes sounds perfectly ok to me!

Jan

From: Judy B | Date: 2004-10-27 12:14:28 UTC
From VA to CT, CO, Dartmoor, CT, and now off to BC/Alberta
Canada. . . is that far enough?
I understand the MMM was estimated at 30 miles for the 130+ boxes, so
I probably did 20 miles for the 95 I got in 3.5 days

But in one day, for clue(series), it is not so much the distance as
the terrain. One Mile in pine paved VA vs rocky CT and level vs
exhausting makes a big difference. So although mile/box/mile/box is
one approach I'd prefer 30min/box/30min/box (which is still about
mile=30min of moderate terrain.)

again how much fun this hobby is?)
But I have to say, I really appreciate having a box at the end of a
long hike to keep me going, otherwise I'd take five steps into the
woods, say, "Very nice. Very pretty. Let's go home." Now I can
say,"gasp.... pant.... must.... gasp... get.... pant... box....."

Ryan might hold the record, he once walked approximately 26 miles for
one letterbox. It was a box along a river in California and you were
supposed to take a boat to it! But he decided to hike it.

If I am going to do 5 miles or more, I prefer it to be a series. When
I find a lone box on a long hike like that, I usually take along a box
to hide on the same trail. This makes it more enjoyable for future box
hunters. Now they have 2 letterboxes on the long trail. :-)

There is one box in the Seattle area called Poo Poo Point. It was only
about 4 miles, but it was up hill all the way. I planted the Catnap
letterbox at about the halfway mark.
