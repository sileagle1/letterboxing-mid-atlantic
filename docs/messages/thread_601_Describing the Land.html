In writing clues it seems I often need to describe the land. As a suburban boy, I find

be a mountain?

That kinda stuff bothers me as I try to describe the way to a box.

Anybody know of a book or online resource for this sort of thing?

Your kind help is most appreciated.

From: Mary from Virginia | Date: 2004-12-01 18:29:49 UTC
There isn't any reason to be so precise. The clue can read "go until
you reach a body of water". Or if you want to be more precise "go
downhill until you reach flowing water"

Creek, river, stream, brook, pool, pond, lake - it doesn't really
matter, does it? What matters is what happens when you get there.

Hill, rise, ridge - Again, what happens when I reach the top or
bottom or whatever?

Path/trail - They are usually used interchangeably. If it makes a
difference, use adjectives, i.e., paved trail, dirt trail, gravel
trail, rocky trail, faint path, wide path, mulch path, grassy path,
path at 75 degrees, path after the bridge... Or skip it all
together "when you come to the third blue blaze..." or "when you come
to the fork..."

Don't make it too hard on yourself. We've all found boxes with clues
even more vague than these.

Mary

I don't want to sound high-handed here, but what I am looking for is a specific resource.

Part of the fun of this clue for me is using very specific accurate language about the
terrain to make the box more challenging. If I send you into the woods and tell you at a
fork to take the path not the trail, I want to be sure I am correct in my directions.

If I tell you to cross the creek, not the stream you might understand that it is dangerous to
cross the streams, but you may not know which direction to head.

If I say I want you to parallel the ridgeline along the dry wash to the gully below the
switchback I don't want you to end up crying at the Ranger station about how you got lost
because Natty Bumppo doesn't know a ravine from a gorge.

Yes, I could write these clues differently, but this is how I choose to write this particular
clue.

The dictionary was a great idea, but even the good ones seem to use certain terms
interchangably with making distictions.

I am no authority, but here is my look on things.
 
Body of water progression: Run<Stream<Brook<Creek<River.  A canal is a man made body of flowing water.
 
A pool is a body of water with no natural source of flowing water, a pond has a natural source of flowing water, like a stream flowing into it.  Both can be man made or natural.  A reservoir is man made and larger then a pool or pond, but generally smaller then a lake (though not always, kinda like the English language, all the rules are not set in stone).
 
A rise is just that, a rise in the land, and usually gradual, while a hill is more like a defines bump in the land.  A ridge is an elongated hill.  If you are huffing and puffing when you get to the top of a hill, then it is not a hill, but instead it is a mountain.
 
A trail has some kind of paving, dirt, gravel, stone, mulch.  A path has no paving, sometimes has marks on trees that it follows, and usually will get you lost.

Some terms from the spouse (honest. I'm only taking dictation):
open bog - No tree cover, filled with herbaceous and woody plants
separated by mud flats or open water. Plant material usually
appearing on hummocks. Sunny.
closed bog - Overstory of trees, understory of ericaceous shrubs,
subtended by sphagnum moss and mixed herbaceous and marginally
aquatic plants.
Bogs are usually marginal to a stream, but do not have water moving
through them
swamp - tree cover, understory of shrubs and sub-canopy trees
separated by corridors of moving water, under which is really
stinking, sucking mud (technical term, there)
spung - looks like a swamp, but the water doesn't move, and it
usually has an irridescent film of oil on the surface, the result of
the decay of vegetation, which is forming that delicious sucking mud.
marsh - Looks like your lawn that hasn't been mowed for months, but
it's full of water and turtles, spotted with severe-looking stalking
birds, comes in both the fresh-water and brackish varieties.
ephemeral pond - most of the year looks like a depression in the
woods, until spring, when it rains cats and dogs, then looks like a
depression in the woods filled with cats and dogs.
dismal swamp - looks like the aforementioned swamp, but darker, and
filled with squeaks and groans from swaying trees, festooned with
impenetrable vines and briars which conceal the ominous beasts that
lurk withing.
bush - a naturally occurring form of vegetation which requires no
maintenance or remembrance (you pass them all the time in the swamp)
shrubbery - resident of a yard or nursery and may be the poorest
example of its genetic code that ever germinated, however it is your
responsibility to make it lovely, fret over it, and to replace it,
at great expense, when it lives up to its limited genetic potential.

Some terms from the spouse (honest. I'm only taking dictation):
open bog - No tree cover, filled with herbaceous and woody plants
separated by mud flats or open water. Plant material usually
appearing on hummocks. Sunny.
closed bog - Overstory of trees, understory of ericaceous shrubs,
subtended by sphagnum moss and mixed herbaceous and marginally
aquatic plants.
Bogs are usually marginal to a stream, but do not have water moving
through them
swamp - tree cover, understory of shrubs and sub-canopy trees
separated by corridors of moving water, under which is really
stinking, sucking mud (technical term, there)
spung - looks like a swamp, but the water doesn't move, and it
usually has an irridescent film of oil on the surface, the result of
the decay of vegetation, which is forming that delicious sucking mud.
marsh - Looks like your lawn that hasn't been mowed for months, but
it's full of water and turtles, spotted with severe-looking stalking
birds, comes in both the fresh-water and brackish varieties.
ephemeral pond - most of the year looks like a depression in the
woods, until spring, when it rains cats and dogs, then looks like a
depression in the woods filled with cats and dogs.
dismal swamp - looks like the aforementioned swamp, but darker, and
filled with squeaks and groans from swaying trees, festooned with
impenetrable vines and briars which conceal the ominous beasts that
lurk withing.
bush - a naturally occurring form of vegetation which requires no
maintenance or remembrance (you pass them all the time in the swamp)
shrubbery - resident of a yard or nursery and may be the poorest
example of its genetic code that ever germinated, however it is your
responsibility to make it lovely, fret over it, and to replace it,
at great expense, when it lives up to its limited genetic potential.
