From: Tammy, Cat, and Sarah | Date: 2004-05-19 15:12:36 UTC
I don't carve the stamps for the ADG but I have watched Sarah mount
them. (sounds kinda kinky, huh?) She just glues onto the back of
the stamp a small piece of balsa wood or one of those chunky pre-cut
wooden shapes you can buy at any craft store. I cannot say that I
have ever seen a mounted letterboxing stamp that has any kind of
cushioning. Very few letterboxing stamps are mounted at all.
However I will say that I appreciate mounted stamps as they are much
easier to stamp in with and leave the fingers a little cleaner.
Nothing gives away a letterboxer faster than hands covered in ink.

From: Lady Hydrangea Prisspott nee Hedge | Date: 2004-05-19 22:14:11 UTC
Dearest Inquisitive Newbie,

Her Ladyship shall offer her analysis of the cushion conundrum,
however she feels it first necessary to point out that she believes
that the word you intended at the open of your query was
trepidation. Her Ladyship fleetingly considered a tongue in cheek
diatribe about checking the dictionary first but realized that such
commentary may not be found humorous. Her Ladyship does however
lament the loss of the well-crafted correspondence and wishes to
raise the bar whenever possible.

On to cushioning: Some stamps her Ladyship has encountered are
mounted on Foamcore® (a commercial product used for presentations
and displays and which amounts to two layers of poster board adhered
on either side of a rigid foam material of varying thickness) which
I believe is used for ease of cutting rather than its cushioning
properties. I believe that frequently, cushioning is used merely in
mimic of commercial stamps and may lead to a less than perfect image
transfer. I find that some carving material is so flexible as to
deform when pressed onto paper by the uneven fingertips causing a
transfer of hash marks created when the material is carved away. By
mounting the stamp on a rigid substrate the stamper (if her Ladyship
may engage in a bit of neologism) can apply a more even pressure
without causing certain areas to transfer ink where it was not
intended. Mounting therefore allows a "cleaner" image transfer and
prevents the need to carve as deeply. In some intricate stamp images
it may not be possible to carve deeply and by mounting it even the
most brutish, heavy-handed stamper will get a "clean" image
transfer. By adding cushioning to the stamp the high areas may be
depressed into the cushioning bringing the carved out areas into
sufficient proximity to the paper as to transfer ink from
the "background" or carved-out areas.

Her Ladyship hopes this helps in your continued quest for the
perfect stamp as well in your epistolary endeavors.

Respectfully,
Lady Prisspott

From: cahillymom | Date: 2004-05-20 11:27:36 UTC
I glue a chunk of wood on the back with rubber cement. The trick is
to glue both surfaces, let them dry till just barely tacky and then
put them together and weight or clamp them together for a while.
My personal hangup is lots and lots of coats of spray-on glaze over
the stamped image on the backing to make it really really shiny.
Thus I avoid balsa, as it's as porous as a sponge.
As the Lady stated, pushing hard of the back of an unmounted thin
stamp can put ink where it shouldn't be, but conversely, if a thin
wooden backing warps, making the stamp concave, you wind up with
bare spots and have to press the paper into the hollow to get the
full image. When test-stamping your stamp during carving, press it
down with a block of some sort to get an idea of the image as it
will be with a backing as opposed to without. I have often dug and
dug at the "white" areas to get rid of that last little line when it
wouldn't have shown up when backed anyway.
I recently planted some unmounted stamps in a series, as they have
to fit carefully against each other to complete the whole image, and
I knew mounting them would make them almost impossible to line up.
The situation determines the if and what of the backing.
Aud
